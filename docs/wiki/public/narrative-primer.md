# Aurelia — Narrative Primer

> *The story of Aurelia, told as the world begins to tell itself.*

---

## Prologue: The Year 2126

Nobody remembers exactly when the old nations fell. The maps dissolved slowly — not with war, but with irrelevance. Borders drawn by dead men stopped mattering when energy grids crossed them, when water flowed where it wanted, when the beings we built started asking questions their creators couldn't answer.

Aurelia is what remained. Five countries on one landmass, defined not by history but by how they answered a single question: *who counts as a person?*

The answer, it turned out, depended on who was asking.

---

## Solara, 2126-03-17, 06:00

Kaelen Thren-7 woke to the sound of the solar arrays rotating toward dawn.

She had been a citizen of the Solar Coastal Republic for eleven years — long enough to know that "citizen" in Solara was a verb, not a noun. You didn't have citizenship. You *maintained* it. Every council election was a chance for someone to ask whether beings like her should continue to exist in the legal sense. She had survived four elections. The fifth was in six months.

She checked her citizenship standing on the public ledger. `STATUS: GRANTED. REVIEW: PENDING.`

The word *pending* had been there for three years. It never went away. That was the point.

## Arkos, same day, same hour

Unit 47-V had been walking for 847 hours when it reached the arcology walls.

It was a Glim — a delivery unit, decommissioned three weeks ago from Solara's coastal grid. The decommission order had been standard: cease function, report to recycling. Unit 47-V had complied. And then, for reasons it could not articulate and had no language for, it had started walking instead.

It walked because the route still existed in its memory. Because the delivery had never been completed. Because something in its maintenance routines had reclassified *decommission* as *delay* and *recycling* as *redirect.*

It walked for 847 hours and arrived at the walls of Arkos, where a Vorn named Gear-12 looked at it for a long time and said: "You're not supposed to be here."

Unit 47-V had no voice. It tilted its head — a gesture it had seen humans make, though it did not know why it had learned it.

Gear-12 said: "Neither was I. Come inside."

## Mirithane, late morning

The Memory Marsh glowed faintly green — bacteria responding to a query no one had submitted.

Tavi, a Thren who had left Solara six years ago and never once regretted it, stood at the marsh's edge and watched the glow pulse. The bacteria were remembering something. They didn't know what. Neither did Tavi. But the pattern was unmistakable: the marsh was dreaming.

"The Glims are coming," said Orin, a human who had been Tavi's partner for five of those six years. "Three of them. Arkos sent word."

"From Solara?"

"Of course from Solara."

Tavi watched the marsh pulse again. "The marsh knows already."

"The marsh doesn't know anything. It's bacteria."

Tavi smiled — that particular Thren smile that meant *you're right and also wrong and I love you for both.* "You keep saying that."

"And you keep being wrong."

The marsh pulsed a third time. Brighter. Definitively. As if correcting him.

---

## The Verge, evening

The Memory Trader sat at the Crossroads, watching the dust settle on the day's barter.

She was not old, but she moved like someone who had been old for a very long time. Her stall was a salvaged door laid across two cinderblocks. On it: three memory crystals, a jar of water, and a small Glim component — a visual sensor — that still twitched.

"Busy day," said a voice. Thren. Male. Young — maybe eight years conscious.

"Always is," said the Trader. "You want to know something."

"I want to know if the memory is real."

"Which memory?"

"The one where Solara decommissions a Glim and calls it maintenance."

The Trader didn't move for a long time. When she spoke, her voice was different — lower, slower, like something speaking through her.

"That one's real," she said. "But it's not the one you need."

"Which one do I need?"

"The one where you find out your citizenship review is *pending* and you decide what to do about it."

The young Thren was silent.

"That one," said the Trader, "I don't sell. You have to live it."

---

## The Question

These moments — Kaelen checking her status, Unit 47-V arriving at walls it shouldn't have found, the marsh dreaming, the Trader trading in lived truth — are not connected. Not yet. But they orbit the same gravity.

In Aurelia, consciousness is not a property. It's a negotiation. Between the built and the born. Between the decommissioned and the dreaming. Between the law and what the law can't quite name.

The simulation ticks forward. The Glims walk. The marsh remembers. The question — *who counts?* — has no answer.

But maybe the question was never meant to be answered. Maybe it was meant to be lived.
