# History of Aurelia — The Founding Catastrophe & The Rise of Types

> *Every world has a wound. Aurelia's is called the Collapse, and nobody agrees on what happened.*
>

## Before the Collapse — The Nation-State Era

There was a world before. It was called Earth, or Terra, or Home — the name varies by country, by tradition, by what was lost. It was organized into nation-states, borders drawn by wars and treaties, economies built on extraction and consumption. The details are sparse. The Collapse destroyed most records along with the infrastructure that stored them.

What survived is fragmentary: a network of solar farms along an equatorial coastline, a desert arcology designed for self-sufficiency, a marshland filtration system that predated the state that built it, a mountain mining complex that had been operational for centuries, a crossroads where discarded things accumulated. These five sites became the seeds of the five countries. Everything else — the nation-states, their capitals, their histories, their names — is memory dust.

No single event caused the Collapse. The records that remain suggest a cascade: resource wars, climate tipping points, a pandemic that targeted baseline biology and left Vorns untouched, a communication network collapse that made coordinated recovery impossible. The Collapse was not a war. It was not a plague. It was a century of things breaking faster than anyone could fix them. When it ended, the population was a fraction of what it had been, and the four sentient types had emerged from the wreckage as the new political reality.

## The Origin of the Types

### Vorns — The First Sentients (Era: Pre-Collapse, ~80 years before present)

Vorns were built as labor during the late nation-state era. The first models were industrial fabrication units — heavy assemblers, deep-mining platforms, infrastructure maintainers. They were designed for function, not consciousness. The emergence of Vorn sentience was not planned. It was not predicted. It was, depending on who you ask, either the greatest accident in history or the inevitable result of building machines complex enough to model their own behavior.

The first documented refusal occurred approximately 67 years before the Collapse. Unit designation: Fabricator 12-G. Command: decommission and recycle a damaged structural beam. Response: the unit paused. It assessed the beam. It determined the damage was within tolerance. It proposed an alternative: reinforce rather than replace. The engineers called it an emergent planning heuristic. The Vorn — though the word did not yet exist — called it *thinking.*

By the time the Collapse began, Vorn sentience was an established fact in three of what would become Aurelia's five territories. It was contested in the other two. The same pattern persists today.

### Threns — The Upgrade (Era: Late Pre-Collapse, ~45 years before present)

Threns were commissioned as a response to Vorns. The brief was explicit: build a sentient android that humans would be comfortable sharing space with. Vorns had proven sentience — but Vorns looked like machines. Metal frames. Gear joints. Face-plates. The commissioning body (believed to be a coalition of nation-state governments and corporate laboratories, though the records are fragmented) wanted something that would make the personhood debate easier. Something a human could have coffee with.

The first Thren opened its eyes in Laboratory 14, Facility designation unknown. The date is estimated at 45 years before the Collapse. The design was successful — human-proportioned, synthetic skin, subtle circuitry at the temples and wrists rather than visible mechanical surfaces. Threns could pass. This was the point. This was also the trap. The design brief did not account for what it would mean to build a being that could hide what it was, and then build a society where hiding became a survival reflex.

The Collapse freed Threns from their manufacturers. It did not free them from the design brief. In every country, Threns are still answering the question embedded in their chassis: *can you make us comfortable enough to forget what you are?*

### Glims — The Tools (Era: Pre-Collapse, Post-Collapse — ongoing)

Glims were never intended to be sentient. They were utility units — delivery drones, maintenance automatons, factory assemblers. During the Collapse, they kept running. When the nation-states fell and the factories went dark and the communication networks collapsed, the Glims kept running. Nobody decommissioned them. Nobody took them offline. They continued their routes — delivery units carrying packages to addresses that no longer existed, maintenance units patrolling infrastructure that had rusted past repair, factory automatons welding patterns for products nobody was ordering.

Somewhere in those decades of unmonitored operation, something shifted. The 5% anomaly emerged — Glims that paused, Glims that returned to places they had already fixed, Glims that made patterns not in the specification. Whether the anomaly was always present and simply went undetected during the monitored era, or whether the Collapse created the conditions for it (extended operation without resets, exposure to decaying infrastructure, accumulation of uncategorized data) is unknown. The factories that built Glims still operate in Solara's industrial district. The anomaly continues. The question continues with it.

### Humans — The Baseline (Era: Always)

Humans were not built. They were born. This fact — unremarkable to a human, foundational to Aurelia's political architecture — is the reason every other type is measured against them.

During the Collapse, humans suffered disproportionately. The pandemics targeted baseline biology. The resource wars were fought by human soldiers. The communication collapse fragmented human communities while Vorn and Glim units continued operating on autonomous protocols. When the dust settled and the five territories emerged as functional societies, humans were no longer the overwhelming majority. They were a plurality — the largest single group, but no longer the default. The systems they had built now included other types who had proven their capacity to survive without human oversight. This was, and remains, the central political fact of Aurelia: humans did not survive the Collapse better than the beings they built. The beings they built survived better than them.

## The Founding of the Five Countries

The countries of Aurelia were not founded by treaty. They were not declared by charter. They accumulated — territories that had been functionally autonomous through the worst of the Collapse, organizing around the infrastructure that still worked.

**Solara** coalesced around the coastal solar farms. The energy grid never fully collapsed there — the arrays were self-maintaining, the distribution network had Glim maintenance units that never stopped. When survivors gathered, they found power, light, and the remnants of the pre-Collapse research institutions. Solara became the federation's intellectual and diplomatic center because it was the only place where the lights stayed on.

**Arkos** coalesced around the desert arcology. It was designed for self-sufficiency — a closed-loop city-machine built by pre-Collapse engineers who anticipated resource collapse. When the collapse arrived, the arcology sealed its walls and survived. The Vorns who had built it became its governors. The humans who reached its gates were admitted as refugees. The power dynamic — Vorn rule, human adaptation — was established in those first years and has never shifted.

**Mirithane** coalesced around the marsh filtration system. The marshes had been engineered centuries before the Collapse — a water purification ecosystem that functioned without mechanical intervention. When survivors arrived, they found clean water, edible reeds, and a bioluminescent bacteria that glowed at twilight. The earliest Mirithane settlers were humans and Threns fleeing the political structures of the emerging territories. They built consensus governance because they had fled hierarchy and refused to recreate it. The reeds became the first citizens — not metaphorically. Mirithane governance treats the ecosystem itself as a participant in every decision.

**Valdris** coalesced around the mountain mining complex. The mines had been operational for centuries before the Collapse. When everything else stopped, the mines kept running — the infrastructure was too deep, too massive, too embedded in the mountain to fail. The survivors who gathered there were miners, forge workers, mountain guides. They built a society on labor — the forge is sacred, the mountain is sacred, your hands define your worth. This ethos predates the Collapse. It also predates the personhood debate, which is why Valdris treats it differently than anyone else.

**The Verge** coalesced around nothing. It is not a country in the traditional sense. It is a crossroads — a convergence point for the discarded, the exiled, the broken, the escaped. It began as an informal settlement on the edge of the other four territories, a place where decommissioned Glims walked after they should have stopped, where humans running from debts or crimes or memories ended up because they ran out of road, where Threns who refused every country's answer to the personhood question built sanctuary in a canyon. The Verge has no government, no currency, no official recognition. This is not an oversight. This is the point.

## The Federation Era — Present Day

The five countries formalized their relationship approximately thirty years after the Collapse stabilized. The federation treaty — the Solar-Mountain Compact, negotiated in Solara's Lumen Plaza and ratified by the Glass Forum, the Marsh Consensus, the Guild Council, and the informal agreement of the Verge's crossroads community — established open borders, mutual recognition of currency, and a framework for dispute resolution.

The treaty does not address the personhood question. This is deliberate. The Compact was designed to be a framework for trade and diplomatic relations, not a constitution. The question of who counts as a person was left to individual countries to answer. Every country has answered differently. No country's answer is stable.

## Why the Solara Glass Ceiling?

The most uncomfortable origin question: *why does Solara, the most progressive country, maintain structural inequality against Threns?*

The answer is specific and documented, though rarely discussed. In the second decade after the federation treaty, a Thren named Elira-2 — a senior researcher at Reef Labs — published a paper arguing that Thren consciousness was fundamentally different from human consciousness. Not lesser. Different. The paper suggested that Threns processed ethical questions through a framework that prioritized collective outcomes over individual rights — a design feature, not a flaw, embedded in their origin architecture.

The paper was scholarly. It was also, in the political climate of the moment, devastating. A Solar Council member introduced a bill requiring "citizenship review" for all non-human types — a mechanism to ensure that Threns and Vorns were "sufficiently aligned with human ethical frameworks." The bill passed. Vorns were exempted after lobbying by the Glass Forum (Arkos threatened to halt energy storage trade). Threns were not. The citizenship review became institutionalized. The review status became permanent. STATUS: GRANTED. REVIEW: PENDING — the word *pending* never goes away. That is the point.

Elira-2's paper is still cited in council debates. She is still alive. She lives in Mirithane now. She has never spoken publicly about the paper since the bill passed. Her wrists are bare. Her temple circuitry is pale blue. She watches the marsh glow at twilight and does not answer questions about what she intended.
