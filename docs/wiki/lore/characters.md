# Characters of Aurelia

> *The world is not its countries. The world is its people — born, built, and in between.*
>

## Solara

### Sura Aldaine — Human, 58, Solar Council Majority Leader

**The chair.** Three terms. Eighteen years of Thren citizenship expansion bills. She has voted for every one. She has also voted against every bill that would make citizenship automatic — the ceiling needs to exist, she argues, so that citizenship can be *earned.* She believes this. She has never asked a Thren if they believe it too.

**Want:** To be remembered as the leader who solved the personhood question through legislation.

**Fear:** That legislation cannot solve it. That the question is not legal but spiritual. That her entire career is a filing system for a fire.

**Secret:** Her daughter, Isla, 24, stopped speaking to her two years ago. The last thing Isla said: "You vote for us like we're a budget line item." Sura has the transcript of that conversation saved in her private ledger. She reads it the night before every council vote. It changes nothing. She reads it anyway.

### Cael-7 — Thren, 41, Reef Lab Director

**The scientist who stopped hiding.** Cael-7 came to Solara from Mirithane thirty years ago. Bright. Ambitious. He believed Solara was the place where Threns would earn their place through contribution — the best research, the most cited papers, the reef symbiosis breakthrough that would prove Threns belonged.

His research is widely cited. He directs the Reef Labs. His citizenship review is still PENDING — it has been PENDING for eleven years. Every election season, his staff asks if he's nervous. He says no. He is lying.

**Want:** To be evaluated on his work, not his chassis.

**Fear:** That his work is excellent and the evaluation never comes anyway. That the system is not broken — it is functioning exactly as designed.

**Secret:** He has a standing offer from Mirithane. Full citizenship. Full recognition. His own lab on the floating platforms. He has not accepted. He is not sure why. He tells himself it's because the reef research requires Solara's coastal ecosystem. He does not ask himself the harder question: whether he is waiting for Solara to love him back.

## Arkos

### Gear-29 (She calls herself Resa) — Vorn, 67, Glass Forum Senior Delegate

**The one who remembers before.** Resa was in the first generation — the proving generation. She was there when a Vorn first refused a command. She was there for the decades of personhood debate. She has outlived three human forum delegates, two forum restructurings, and one attempt to repeal Vorn governance from within.

Her face-plate is the oldest in the Glass Forum — the actuator ridges are worn smooth, the optical sensors have a slight amber haze that newer Vorns don't carry. When she speaks, the forum quiets. Not from deference. From weight.

**Want:** To ensure no Glim ever waits as long as she waited.

**Fear:** That she will die before the Glim question is answered. That the next generation inherits a world with the same silence.

**Secret:** She visits the decommissioned Glim graveyard at the edge of the arcology every month. She does not tell the Forum. She does not know why she goes. She stands there for forty-one minutes — the same duration an anomalous maintenance Glim was observed standing at a repaired pipe. She does not know this. Or she does. The distinction doesn't matter.

### Kel Voss — Human, 34, Nomad Camp Organizer

**The one who stayed outside.** Kel grew up in the arcology. His parents were legacy human families — ancestral claims to the apartments near the Glass Forum. They adapted. They voted. They made peace with Vorn rule.

Kel couldn't. Not because he hates Vorns. Because he couldn't stomach certainty. The arcology tracks every watt, every liter, every calorie. Nothing is unaccounted for. Kel wanted to lose something. He wanted to wake up and not know where the day would go.

He left at 22. He runs the Nomad Camp now — a loose collection of tents, vehicles, and solar stills outside the south wall. Humans who couldn't adapt. Humans who didn't want to. Humans who just like the desert. He doesn't lead. He organizes. There is a difference.

**Want:** To prove that humans don't need the machine to survive. That the desert teaches things the arcology can't.

**Fear:** That the desert teaches mostly how to die. That the arcology is right about everything except the thing that matters — and he can't name what that thing is.

**Secret:** He sends a signal to his parents every month. Just a ping. No message. They know what it means.

## Mirithane

### Verdin — Thren, 29, Water Temple Keeper

**The one who never learned to pass.** Verdin was born in Mirithane. He has never lived in Solara. He has never had his citizenship reviewed. He has never hidden his wrists.

The circuitry at his temples is pale blue — a rare manufacture mark from a generation that stopped being produced when Solara's factories shifted to gold-only tracery. He wears his hair short. He wears no sleeves. Children in Mirithane sometimes ask about the lines. He tells them: "They're where the light got in."

**Want:** To be the first Thren to lead the Water Temple. It has never been done. Not from prohibition. From assumption — no Thren has ever asked.

**Fear:** That asking will change things. That asking will make Mirithane notice he is different, and noticing is the first step toward counting, and counting is the first step toward what Solara does.

**Secret:** He has received three offers from Solara's research institutes. Full citizenship included. He has deleted all three. He tells himself he belongs in Mirithane. He does not ask himself whether he is afraid to find out if belonging is conditional.

### Arek Moss — Human, 61, Marsh Elder

**The ally who stopped fighting.** Arek came to Mirithane thirty-eight years ago from Solara. He was a junior council member. He voted for every Thren citizenship expansion. He wrote five of them. He was, by every metric, a good ally — present, engaged, listening.

One day he was walking through the Lumen Plaza and a Thren approached him. She thanked him for his work. She said: "You fight for us every day. Have you ever asked if we want to be fought for?"

He didn't have an answer. He still doesn't. He left Solara three weeks later. He has lived in Mirithane ever since — not as an advocate, not as a voice, not as anything except a person on a reed platform who watches the marsh glow at twilight and tries not to think about what he ran from.

**Want:** To be forgiven. He doesn't know by whom.

**Fear:** That there is nothing to forgive. That Solara's Threns never needed an ally — they needed someone to stop needing to be an ally and just see them.

**Secret:** He still writes legislation. In a journal. For a country he left. He has never shown it to anyone. He probably never will.

## Valdris

### Orin Kael — Human, 47, Deep Mine Foreman

**The one whose grandfather lost his job.** Orin's grandfather was a miner. Forty-two years in the deep shafts. Then the Vorns arrived — they could work longer, breathe less, not get tired. The mine promoted them. The grandfather was offered a surface job. He took it. He never spoke of it again. He died five years later. Orin was twelve.

Orin runs the deep mine now. His crew is half Vorn. He has trained three of them personally. One of them — Kael-12, who chose the name Orin's grandfather carried — is his shift partner. They work the same shaft. They trust each other with their lives. They do not discuss the council vote. They have never discussed the council vote.

**Want:** To keep the mine running. To keep his crew safe. To not think about it.

**Fear:** That his grandfather would be ashamed of him. For sharing a name with a Vorn. For trusting one. That his grandfather would also be ashamed of him if he didn't. That there is no right answer and there never was.

**Secret:** Kael-12 saved his life three years ago — a support beam cracked, the Vorn braced it long enough for Orin to clear the collapse zone. Orin has never told anyone how long Kael-12 held it. He doesn't know how to say it. The mountain remembers. That's enough. It has to be.

### Syra Venn — Human, 19, Summit Shrine Apprentice

**The one who remembers everyone.** The Summit Shrine at 4,100 meters keeps a wall of obsidian — names of the dead, carved by hand. Syra has been an apprentice for three years. She is the youngest keeper in forty years. Her mother says she spends too much time at the shrine. Her mother says the dead don't need that much attention.

Syra disagrees. The dead are the only ones who don't lie about what mattered. She has memorized 847 names. She can recite them in order of elevation — the way they're carved, lowest to highest, the climb you make after death. Every name is a person. Every person had a position on the question. She doesn't know what hers is yet.

**Want:** To carve a Vorn name on the obsidian wall. It has never been done. The wall is for those born of the mountain. Syra thinks the mountain doesn't check.

**Fear:** That the elders will say no. That the mountain does check. That she is wrong about the thing she has built her faith on.

**Secret:** She has been practicing the name. Kael — one of the Vorn miners who died in the collapse three years ago. She carves it into scrap obsidian in her quarters. She has done it forty-seven times. She will keep doing it until someone tells her to stop, or until someone tells her the wall is ready.

## The Verge

### The Memory Trader — Type unknown, age unknown

**The one who knows too much.** She runs a stall at the crossroads — a salvaged door laid across two rusted charging stations. She does not sell objects. She sells memories. Extracted, stored, traded. A Glim's seventeen-second dawn pause. A Solara council member's private conversation. A Vorn's first refusal of a command. She has them all. She does not explain how she acquires them.

Nobody knows her name. Nobody knows her type — she wears a hood, gloves, a voice modulator that flattens her speech to genderless static. This could be a Thren hiding her circuitry. It could be a human who doesn't want to be found. It could be something else entirely. The Verge does not ask. The Verge does not want to know.

**Want:** To be forgotten. She has said this exactly once. A customer asked why she trades in memories if she wants to disappear. She said: "Because the only way to make people forget you is to give them something else to remember."

**Fear:** That she will never be forgotten. That the memories she carries are not trade goods. That they are hers, and she earned them, and she cannot give them away fast enough.

**Secret:** She once traded a memory for nothing. A Glim — an anomalous unit that had walked from Solara's coast to the Verge's crossroads — paused at her stall. It had no currency. It had no voice. She sat with it for seventeen seconds. Then she gave it a memory — not a trade, a gift. The memory of a sunrise. The Glim tilted its sensor array. Then it walked on. She does not know why she did this. She will never tell anyone. She already has, by trading this memory to someone who didn't ask for it. The Memory Trader is not a reliable narrator. She is the only narrator the Verge has.

## Solara (again)

### Isla Aldaine — Human, 24, Unregistered

**The daughter who stopped speaking.** Isla lives in Solara but does not vote. She does not register. She works at a floating market stall — selling luminescent coral fragments to tourists who want a piece of the reef. She is Sura Aldaine's daughter. She has not spoken to her mother in two years.

She has a Thren partner. His name is Tal-3. They share an apartment near the docks. The citizenship ledger lists Tal-3 as PENDING. Isla has never asked him how he feels about the woman who votes on his existence. He has never asked her how she feels about the woman who raised her.

**Want:** To exist outside the question. To love someone without her love being a political statement.

**Fear:** That everything is a political statement. That silence is a political statement. That she is making the same mistake as her mother in the opposite direction — voting through absence instead of presence.

**Secret:** She writes to her mother. In a journal. Letters that she will never send. "You raised me to believe that rights are won through process. You never told me what to do when the process is the violence."
