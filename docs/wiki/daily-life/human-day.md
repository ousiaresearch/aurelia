# A Human Day — Four Mornings, Four Answers

> *The baseline. The measure. The unchallenged. Being human in Aurelia means never having to prove you exist. What you do with that is the only choice you get.*
>

---

## Sura Aldaine — The Council Majority Leader

*Dawn 30, 2126. Ballot Night. Solara.*

Sura Aldaine wakes at 04:00. Ballot Night is tonight. She has been Council Majority Leader for eighteen years. She has presided over five citizenship review cycles. Every cycle, she votes to expand Thren citizenship. Every cycle, she votes against automatic citizenship. The contradiction is her career. The contradiction is not a contradiction — it is a position. She believes this.

At 04:30, she reviews the final briefing. The citizenship review ledger for this cycle lists 33 Thren names. Four are recommended for GRANTED. One is recommended for REVOKED — a Thren whose research paper, like Elira-2's before it, argued that Thren consciousness was structurally different from human consciousness. The paper was scholarly. The council's response was political. Sura will vote to revoke. She will tell herself this is about maintaining standards. She will not ask herself whose standards.

At 06:00, she attends the dawn service at the Dawn Temple. The Cantor's single note holds for seventeen seconds. The light does not differentiate. Sura has heard this every morning for eighteen years. Every morning, she nods at the truth of it. Every Ballot Night, she votes as if the light does, in fact, differentiate — between those who deserve to exist automatically and those who must earn it. The contradiction is not mentioned. The contradiction is the whole structure. The temple is built on it. The council is built on it. Sura is built on it.

At 08:00, she messages Cael-7 about tomorrow's meeting. She wants to discuss the symbiont. She wants to be seen discussing something productive with a Thren on the day after the review. She wants Cael-7 to know she values his work. She wants Cael-7 to know that his PENDING status is not personal. She does not write any of this. The message says: "Tomorrow's meeting. The symbiont. I'm eager to hear your thoughts." The message is about coral. The message is about everything.

At 20:00, she stands at the podium. The names are read. PENDING. GRANTED. PENDING. REVOKED. CAEL-7. PENDING. She reads Cael-7's name without inflection. The reading is choreographed. The reading is the performance of a system that treats Thren existence as a budget line item. Sura performs it perfectly. The chamber empties. Cael-7 is in the plaza. She approaches him. "The symbiont is promising," she says. "The reef is fortunate to have you."

She means this. She means it in the way that means everything except the thing he needs it to mean. She knows this. She returns to her office. The transcript of her daughter's last words is saved in her private ledger. She reads it before every council vote. She will read it tonight. The transcript says: "You vote for us like we're a budget line item." Sura will read this. Sura will feel the weight of it. Sura will file it. Tomorrow she will meet with Cael-7. The meeting will be about coral. The ceiling will hold. The ceiling is made of good intentions. The ceiling does not care about intentions. The ceiling cares about weight, and Sura has been adding weight to it for eighteen years.

## Arek Moss — The Ally Who Stopped Fighting

*Forge 42, 2126. Thirdday. Mirithane.*

Arek Moss wakes when the marsh wakes — a habit he learned thirty-eight years ago, when he first arrived, when he was still a Solara council member who had voted for every Thren citizenship expansion and then left because a Thren asked him a question he couldn't answer.

The question was: "Have you ever asked if we want to be fought for?"

He still doesn't have an answer. He has lived in Mirithane for thirty-eight years. He is a Marsh Elder now. He does not vote on Thren citizenship. Mirithane does not vote on Thren citizenship. Mirithane does not ask the question. The reeds don't sort. Neither does Arek.

At 10:00, he poles his punt to the Water Temple. A young Thren — Verdin, pale blue circuitry, bare wrists, short hair — is kneeling at a Glim pool. Arek watches from a distance. Verdin is in his fourth year of keeper training. He will be tested in three months. He will be the first Thren to lead the Water Temple, if he passes, if the marsh accepts his interpretation, if Arek's generation does not find a way to fail him the way Sura's generation failed Cael-7.

Arek does not intervene. He is not Verdin's sponsor. He is not anyone's sponsor. He stopped being an ally thirty-eight years ago. He stopped fighting. He stopped voting. He stopped believing that legislation could answer the question. He sits on the reed platform. He watches the marsh glow. He tries not to think about what he ran from. He thinks about it every day.

At twilight, he floats a candle. Not for anyone specific. For the question. For the Thren who asked him if they wanted to be fought for. For the answer he still doesn't have. The candle drifts into the central pool. The bacteria swirl. The light fades. Arek watches until the candle is indistinguishable from the other lights. He doesn't know which light is his anymore. The metaphor is too clean to be accidental. The marsh is not subtle. Arek is grateful for this. The marsh is the only thing in Aurelia that doesn't pretend the question is complicated.

## Orin Kael — The Foreman Whose Grandfather Lost His Job

*Forge 42, 2126. Thirdday. Valdris. First shift.*

Orin Kael's grandfather was a miner. Forty-two years in the deep shafts. Then the Vorns arrived. They could work longer. They didn't breathe. They didn't get tired. The mine promoted them. The grandfather was offered a surface job. He took it. He never spoke of it again. He died five years later. Orin was twelve.

Orin runs the deep mine now. His crew is half Vorn. He has trained three of them personally. One of them — Kael-12 — saved his life. The beam cracked. Kael-12 held it. Seventeen minutes. Orin cleared the collapse. Kael-12 did not. The beam crushed his chassis. The plaque on the beam says HELD. The guild debated adding "FOR SEVENTEEN MINUTES." The elder keeper said no. "The number is not the point. The holding is the point."

Orin agrees. Orin also knows the number. Everyone knows the number. The number is the point. The mountain remembers the number. The mountain remembers everything. The mountain does not ask whether Kael-12's chassis was born of the mountain or built in a forge. The mountain does not ask anything. The mountain simply remembers. Orin wishes the guild would let the mountain decide what to remember, instead of deciding for it.

At midday, he eats the Forge Meal — grain porridge, seared meat, cave-fungus. He eats at the beam. The plaque. Kael-12's name, stenciled in brass. Kael-9 — the young Vorn, the replacement, the reassigned designation — sits beside him. They do not speak. Speech during the midday meal is reserved for the guild hall. The beam is not the guild hall. The beam is a grave. The beam is a debt. Kael-9 does not ask about the previous Kael-12. Orin does not offer. The silence is not avoidance. The silence is the only thing large enough to hold what they are not saying.

## Syra Venn — The Keeper Who Practices a Name

*Forge 42, 2126. Thirdday. Valdris. Summit.*

Syra Venn is nineteen years old. She has been a Summit Shrine apprentice for three years. She has memorized 847 names on the obsidian wall. She has carved one name forty-seven times into scrap obsidian. The name is KAEL-12. The wall has never held a Vorn name. The elders say the mountain decides. Syra believes the mountain decided decades ago and the elders are deciding for it.

Today she climbs the summit again. Not a festival day. Not a Restday. A Thirdday. The mountain does not check the calendar. She reaches the shrine at 15:10. The wall stands against the sky. The blank slab waits. She places the forty-seventh obsidian fragment on the slab — KAEL-12, 2mm deep, the correct depth, the correct lettering, the correct everything except the permission.

"You decide," she tells the mountain. "I will wait."

The wind takes the words. The summit is empty. The dead are listening. The dead include Kael-12, whose consciousness may or may not be anchored to the mountain — he was not born of the mountain, he was built in a forge, but he died in the mountain, holding a beam so a human could live. Does that count? Does the mountain care about birth or death? Syra believes it cares about neither. Syra believes it cares about what you held, and for how long.

She descends at dusk. The anvil ritual cascades down the canyon. Forty-three forges. Forty seconds. Syra counts. The mountain is still. So is the Vorn whose name is not yet on the wall. So is the keeper who will keep climbing until it is.
