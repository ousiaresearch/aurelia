# Dusk at the Crossroads — The Verge Cycle

*Dusk 88, 2126. Restday. The Memory Trader opens her stall. Nobody knows why.*

---

The crossroads do not wake. They accumulate.

At 17:00, a decommissioned Glim arrives from the eastern route — the path that leads toward Solara. Its unit ID is partially scraped off. Its chest LED blinks amber. It has been walking for an unknown duration. It stops at the center of the crossroads. Its sensor array tilts — scanning, or resting, or something that is neither scan nor rest but has no name in the manual. It stands motionless. The crossroads accept it.

At 17:30, the Memory Trader opens her stall. The salvaged door comes down from the two rusted charging stations that serve as its base. She arranges the memory crystals — seven today, down from twelve yesterday, up from the three she had when the month began. Her inventory fluctuates. She does not explain the fluctuations. She sits behind the door, hood up, gloves on, voice modulator humming at standby. The stall is open. The Verge does not have business hours. The Memory Trader's stall opens when she opens it. It closes when she closes it. Nobody has ever seen her arrive. Nobody has ever seen her leave. The door is simply there, or not.

At 18:00, a Thren arrives from the western route — the path that leads toward Mirithane. Their circuitry is painted over in dark pigment, deliberately obscured. Not hidden. Erased. The paint is old, chipped at the edges, revealing gold tracery beneath. They do not approach the Memory Trader's stall. They sit at the crossroads center, near the Glim. The Glim does not acknowledge them. The Thren does not speak. They sit together for forty-one minutes. The Glim's chest LED blinks amber. The Thren watches. The crossroads watch back.

At 19:00, the communal stew pot ignites. Whoever lit it — a human, probably, one of the long-timers who has been here longer than anyone can trace — does not announce themselves. The pot bubbles. The smell of vat-protein and wild onion drifts across the crossroads. People emerge from shelters — salvaged tents, a hollowed drone casing, a lean-to against the Memory Trader's stall that she has never objected to. They gather at the pot. They ladle stew into whatever container they carry. They do not pay. The Verge does not charge for food. The pot is never empty. This is statistically improbable. The Verge does not investigate.

The Memory Trader does not eat. She has never been seen to eat. The glass of water at her stall is for customers. The stew pot is for everyone else. The hood does not lift. The voice modulator does not activate. She sits. She waits. The waiting is the profession.

At 21:00, a human approaches her stall. Young. Scarred at the jaw. Wearing Valdris mining leathers — old, patched, the forge mark faded to illegibility. He sits on the charging station opposite her door. He does not speak for a long time. The Memory Trader waits. She always waits.

"I want to forget the collapse," he says finally. "Section 3-B. Three years ago. I was there. A Vorn held the beam. I didn't help. I ran. I want to forget running."

The Memory Trader tilts her head. The hood does not shift. The voice modulator activates — genderless static, flattened tone, no warmth, no cold. "What do you remember about running?"

"I remember the sound of the beam cracking. I remember the Vorn saying nothing. I remember my boots on the stone. I remember reaching the lift and not looking back."

"Do you remember anything else?"

He is silent. Then: "I remember the Vorn's face-plate. It was... calm. The actuator ridges were still. It wasn't afraid. Or it was, and the face-plate wasn't showing it. I don't know which is worse."

The Memory Trader reaches into a compartment beneath the door. She withdraws a crystal — small, pale amber, stored separately from the others. "This is a memory of a different collapse. The same beam. The same moment. It belonged to the human who was trapped — the one the Vorn held the beam for. He traded it to me two years ago. He wanted to forget the guilt. I do not have the Vorn's memory. The Vorn never came to me."

The man stares at the crystal. "You're giving me his memory?"

"I am offering a trade. Your memory of running for his memory of being saved. You will know what it felt like to be the one who couldn't move, while someone else held the beam. He no longer knows what that felt like. You will carry it for him."

"What do you get?"

"The memory of running. Someone will want it someday. Someone who needs to know what it feels like to survive when someone else didn't. The trade is the point. The memory is the currency."

He takes the crystal. He holds it to his temple — the standard transfer point for humans, though the Memory Trader's method works on contact anywhere. The crystal glows. The transfer completes. His eyes close. He is silent for seventeen seconds. When he opens his eyes, they are wet. The memory of running is gone. The memory of being saved has taken its place. He does not thank her. The Memory Trader does not expect thanks. The trade is complete.

At midnight, the crossroads settle. The stew pot is banked. The long-timers retreat to their shelters. The Thren from Mirithane is still sitting beside the Glim. The Glim's chest LED has shifted to green — a phantom charge, a signal from a battery that should have died years ago. The Memory Trader's stall is still open. It will close before dawn. Or it won't. The Verge does not keep hours. The Verge does not keep anything except whatever people leave behind.

---

*"Still moving. Still here. The crossroads accept everything. The crossroads answer nothing."*
