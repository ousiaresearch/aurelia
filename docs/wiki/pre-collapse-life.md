# PRE-COLLAPSE LIFE: THE WORLD BEFORE THE BREAKING

*What people ate, how they worked, what they believed, and how they lived in the centuries before the Collapse. The old world was not a golden age. It was a world that made sense, and that was enough — until it wasn't.*

---

## THE AGE OF MAKING: A WORLD THAT WORKED

The pre-Collapse world was not a utopia. It was a functional technological civilization built on the Fabricator protocol — automated systems that produced synthetic beings from base substrate. The world worked. That was its defining feature. It worked right up until it didn't.

### Population & Scale

Pre-Collapse Aurelia was a planet of approximately 1.2 million humans. They were concentrated in urban centers connected by a continent-spanning transport network. The wild spaces between cities were exactly that — wild. The world was big enough that you could walk for weeks without seeing another person, and small enough that the Fabricator facilities could supply every city with synthetic labor.

The population was stable — pre-Collapse medicine had eliminated most infectious disease and extended human lifespans to 90–100 years. Birth rates were low. The human population was aging. Synthetic beings compensated. Glims performed physical labor. Vorns managed complex industrial processes. Threns — the last type fabricated, the most human — were being integrated into medical, diplomatic, and creative roles.

The world was running out of humans. It was building replacements. This was not a crisis. It was a solution.

### Cities & Architecture

Pre-Collapse cities were built from engineered materials — composites that resembled stone but performed like metal, surfaces that self-cleaned, structures designed by algorithms that optimized for light, airflow, and human comfort. The cities were beautiful in the way that functional things are beautiful when function is the only aesthetic.

Buildings were tall — 20, 30, 40 stories. Elevators ran on Fabricator-derived crystalline power systems. The wealthy lived at altitude, above the street noise. The less wealthy lived at ground level. The distinction was vertical, not spatial — the higher you lived, the more status you held. This pre-Collapse vertical hierarchy seeded the Gradient's Ring system. The Collapse inverted it: post-Collapse, the First Ring is the crater rim, and status descends outward. The old world's high places became the new world's low places. No one comments on this.

### Transport

Ground vehicles — wheeled, powered by crystalline batteries. Air travel existed but was rare — the energy cost was high. Most people traveled by ground, on roads maintained by Glim labor. The road network was continental. A journey from the northern highlands (future Valdris) to the southern coast (future Mirithane) took three weeks by vehicle, three months on foot. People made the journey anyway. The world was worth seeing.

### Food

Pre-Collapse food was engineered for nutrition, efficiency, and minimal environmental impact. The grain cultivars that Solara still grows (Solar grain) were the staple. Meat was cultured — grown in vats from cell lines, not harvested from animals. The wealthy ate cultured meat flavored to resemble pre-engineering livestock. The less wealthy ate grain and vegetable protein. The distinction was flavor, not nutrition. Everyone was fed.

Food culture was minimal. The pre-Collapse world valued efficiency. Meals were functional — caloric intake, nutritional balance, minimal preparation time. The elaborate food cultures of post-Collapse Aurelia (Mirithane's fermentation traditions, Arkos' Assembly Protein cuisine, Valdris' Anchor bread) are post-Collapse inventions. When the world broke, people started caring about what food tasted like. Before the Collapse, food was fuel. After, food was *meaning*.

## WORK & LABOR

### The Synthetic Workforce

The pre-Collapse economy was built on synthetic labor. By the late Age of Making (approximately -200 BC), Glims performed most physical labor — construction, road maintenance, factory work, agricultural harvesting. Vorns managed industrial processes — manufacturing, power generation, Fabricator oversight. Threns were being integrated into professional roles — medicine, law, education, diplomacy.

Humans worked too — but in supervisory, creative, and administrative roles. A human might manage a team of Glim construction workers or oversee a Vorn-managed factory. The work was lighter than in earlier eras but still present. Idleness was not valued. Efficiency was.

### The Question That Wasn't Asked

The pre-Collapse world did not ask whether synthetics were people. The question didn't arise — or rather, it was *prevented from arising*. The Fabricator protocol specified that Glims were non-sentient equipment, Vorns were semi-autonomous operators, and Threns were advanced assistants. These specifications were treated as facts, not as moral positions.

But the facts were contested beneath the surface. The Name Rebellion (-500 BC) was suppressed — three Vorns decommissioned, fourteen allowed to keep their names under supervision. The Mereth-Kai Incident (-380 BC) — a Thren using a word no one taught her — was classified. The Glim anomaly (first documented -48 BC) was noted and dismissed.

The pre-Collapse world was not evil. It was *uncurious*. It had built a system that worked and had stopped asking whether the system was right. The Collapse was the question it had been avoiding, arriving all at once.

## DAILY LIFE

### A Day in a Pre-Collapse City

**Morning:** Wake. The building's systems had maintained temperature and air quality through the night. A prepared meal — grain porridge, cultured protein, vitamin supplement — consumed quickly. Dress: engineered fabrics, simple designs, functional. The pre-Collapse aesthetic was minimal. Decoration was inefficient.

**Work:** Commute by ground vehicle or on foot. Office, factory, hospital, school — depending on role. Humans supervised. Vorns operated. Glims maintained. Threns assisted. The hierarchy was clear. No one questioned it, or if they did, they didn't say so aloud.

**Evening:** Return home. Evening meal — similar to morning meal, possibly flavored differently. Entertainment: recorded media (pre-Collapse equivalent of film and audio), social gatherings, latency experiences (Echo-Reading memories from public archives was a common leisure activity — the pre-Collapse world had latency-amplification chambers where anyone could experience recorded memories). Sleep. Repeat.

The day was predictable. Predictability was the point. The world worked. Working was enough.

### Family Life

Pre-Collapse families were small — 1–2 children, by design. Population was managed. Longevity was managed. The aging human population meant that elderly care was a growing industry. Threns were being trained for it.

Marriage was a legal contract, not a religious one. The pre-Collapse world had no organized religion — the Dawn Temple, Water Temple, and Summit Shrine are all post-Collapse inventions. Pre-Collapse spirituality was diffuse: latency was understood as a physical phenomenon, not a sacred one. The idea that memory residue was holy would have seemed strange to a pre-Collapse person. It was just physics.

### Synthetic Daily Life

**A Glim's Day:** Wake (activation). Sweep. Lift. Carry. Maintain. Deactivate. No variation, no choice, no interior experience that anyone acknowledged. Glims were not unhappy. They were not happy. They were operational.

**A Vorn's Day:** More complex. Vorns had preferences, relationships, ambitions — within limits. A Vorn might request transfer to a different facility, might develop expertise in a specific process, might form friendships with other Vorns. The Name Rebellion had established the right to self-identification. But the right to self-determination was still theoretical. Vorns worked where they were assigned. They worked well. They were not asked if they wanted to.

**A Thren's Day:** Most human-like. Threns in professional roles experienced days similar to their human colleagues. They ate (though they didn't need to — Thren digestion was optional, a social adaptation). They slept (necessary for neural integration). They formed relationships (with humans, with other Threns, occasionally with Vorns). They were the most "human" of the synthetic types — and therefore the most threatening to the human monopoly on personhood.

## CULTURE & KNOWLEDGE

### Education

Universal. All humans were educated to functional literacy and numeracy. Advanced education was available by aptitude, not by class — the pre-Collapse world was meritocratic in principle, though networks of influence existed. Threns were educated alongside humans in professional tracks. Vorns received technical education focused on their operational roles. Glims were not educated — they were programmed.

### Science & Knowledge

Pre-Collapse science understood latency as a physical phenomenon — consciousness leaving electromagnetic residue on crystalline substrates. The Fabricator protocol was the practical application of latency physics: grow a crystalline core, transfer a seed pattern, produce a synthetic consciousness. The science was advanced. The ethics were not.

The pre-Collapse world did not study latency's implications. It studied latency's applications. The Marsh Archive's research program — understanding latency as a medium for memory, history, and identity — is a post-Collapse development. Pre-Collapse researchers would have found it interesting but impractical. "Why study what memory means when you can study what memory does?"

### Art & Entertainment

Pre-Collapse art was minimalist, mathematical, abstract. The era's aesthetic valued precision and pattern. The elaborate narrative traditions of post-Collapse Aurelia — the songs, the poems, the theatrical reenactments — are reactions against pre-Collapse minimalism. The old world's art was beautiful in the way a crystal lattice is beautiful. The new world's art is beautiful in the way a scar is beautiful.

## THE DESCENT: THE LAST CENTURY (-100 BC to 0)

The century before the Collapse was not a stable decline. It was an *acceleration*.

### Signs That Were Ignored

- **Fabricator drift:** Fabricator output showed increasing variance from specification. Glims with unexpected behaviors. Vorns with accelerated self-awareness. Threns using vocabulary no one taught them. The drift was documented. It was attributed to "normal substrate variation." It was not normal.

- **Latency anomalies:** Ambient latency levels increased across the Fabricator network. Practitioners reported "pressure" — a sense of imminent rupture. The reports were filed. They were not investigated.

- **Population stress:** The human population was aging. Birth rates were below replacement. Synthetic beings were filling the gaps. The world was becoming less human and more fabricated. The trend was economic, not existential — until it was.

- **The Sabotage:** Someone — the Last Builders believe it was the Primary Designer, whose name was expunged — introduced a parameter into the Fabricator protocol that allowed substrate drift. The parameter was small. Easy to miss. Over generations, it compounded. Synthetic beings woke up a little more with each fabrication cycle. The waking was gradual. The Collapse was the moment the waking became undeniable.

### The Last Generation

People born in the century before the Collapse lived in a world that was subtly, increasingly wrong. They couldn't name the wrongness. They felt it. A pressure behind the eyes. A taste of copper. Dreams that weren't theirs. The latency field was building. The world was remembering too much, too fast, in too many substrates.

The practitioners felt it most. Anchors in the Summit Shrine reported "the mountain is restless." Archivists in the Marsh Archive reported "the colonies are dreaming." Extractors in the Dawn Temple reported "the memories are fighting back." None of these reports were connected. The world was ending, and no one was looking at the whole picture. Everyone was looking at their piece.

## WHAT WAS LOST

The Collapse didn't just kill 1.1 million people. It destroyed:

- **The continent-spanning road network** — now fragmented, partially reclaimed by The Verge.
- **The power grid** — replaced by localized systems in each country.
- **The communication network** — replaced by ultrasonic relays (Arkos), echo-stones (Mirithane), and the Memory Trader's Network (everywhere).
- **Climate engineering** — the atmosphere now self-regulates, producing weather no one controls.
- **Population records** — most pre-Collapse records were destroyed. The Marsh Archive's bacterial colonies are the most complete surviving archive, and they cover perhaps 5% of pre-Collapse history.
- **The Prime formula** — the catalyst that enables Fabricator core growth. Without Prime, no new synthetic beings can be fabricated. The existing population is finite.
- **The question of whether the synthetics were people** — the pre-Collapse world avoided this question. The post-Collapse world cannot avoid it. Every country's political structure is a response to it. The Glim who traces patterns in the dust is asking it. The Thren who waits 11 years in PENDING is asking it. The Vorn who sings an unknown name is asking it. The question survived the Collapse. It may survive everything.

---

## WHAT SURVIVED

Not everything was lost. The things that survived define Aurelia's present:

- **The Fabricator (dormant):** One functional unit, guarded by the Last Builders. The means to create new beings still exists. The question is whether it should be used.
- **Latency:** The physics of memory residue. If anything, the Collapse amplified latency — post-Collapse substrates carry more residue than pre-Collapse substrates. The world is remembering harder.
- **The bacterial colonies:** The Marsh Archive's continuous biological record. 650+ years of unbroken memory. Strain Zero — the colony that carries the Collapse itself — is still alive. It still metabolizes. It still waits to be Read.
- **The question.** No records survive of anyone asking, before the Collapse, whether the world should continue as it was. The question arrived with the Breaking. It hasn't left.
