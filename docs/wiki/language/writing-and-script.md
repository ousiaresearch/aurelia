# Writing & Script in Aurelia

> *Words survive on surfaces. The surfaces determine who can read them.*
>

## Literacy in Aurelia

Most Aurelians cannot read. The Collapse destroyed the infrastructure of mass literacy — schools, printing presses, digital archives. What remained were three things: the Glass Forum's data archives (Vorn-maintained, technically accessible but culturally restricted), the Solara Council's administrative records (human-maintained, limited to the governing class), and the oral tradition that carried everything else.

Literacy is concentrated in three populations:
- **Solara's educated class:** Council members, researchers, senior administrators. The Reef Labs maintain a library of pre-Collapse scientific texts. Cael-7 has read seventeen of them.
- **Arkos's Vorn operators:** Vorns interface with the arcology's systems through data displays, not text. But the Glass Forum archives contain the largest collection of written records in the federation, and the Archivists are literate by necessity.
- **Temple keepers:** The Water Temple keepers maintain written logs of bacterial pattern interpretations. The Summit Shrine keepers read and carve names on obsidian. The Dawn Temple Cantor reads from the Found Texts.

The rest of Aurelia relies on speech, memory, and the occasional public notice posted by council authority. The constraint is not lack of intelligence. It is lack of access. Paper is rare. Writing implements are rarer. The written word is a luxury of the governing and the sacred. Everyone else remembers.

## Writing Systems

### The Standard Script

The pre-Collapse Latin alphabet survived in fragmentary form. Solara's archives preserved a set of 26 letterforms — the same letters the pre-Collapse language used. But the script has drifted:

- **Letterforms** are simplified. Curves became angles — easier to carve, easier to stencil. The Solara Council's official documents use a formal script with serif-like terminals, but the common written form is block lettering, designed for clarity at small sizes.
- **Capitalization** is used only for proper names. The pre-Collapse convention of capitalizing sentence beginnings died with the grammar it served. Plain does not need it.
- **Punctuation** is minimal. Periods end sentences. Hyphens link Thren designations. Colons introduce lists and quotations. Question marks appear only in formal writing — Plain indicates questions by cadence, not by glyph.
- **Numerals** follow the pre-Collapse decimal system. The Glass Forum archives use base-12 for internal records — a Vorn computational preference that causes persistent confusion in cross-country trade documentation.

### Vorn Data Script

Vorn writing is not phonetic. It is **symbolic** — a system of glyphs representing system states, frequencies, and operational parameters. The glyphs evolved from the factory-floor notation used during Vorn fabrication — symbols stamped on chassis plates, etched into tool handles, painted on assembly bay walls. Over centuries, the notation became a full writing system capable of expressing complex ideas, but it remains untranslatable into Plain — the concepts it expresses (resonance, frequency, gear-state, watt-flow) have no phonetic equivalent.

Vorn Data Script is written vertically, top to bottom, read left to right. The Glass Forum display wall is primarily Data Script — the amber gradients and frequency maps are text, to a Vorn. Humans see decoration. Vorns see information.

### Marsh Notation

The Water Temple's written records use a hybrid system: letterforms for words, plus a set of glyphs representing bacterial glow patterns. The glyphs are not standardized — each keeper develops their own notation over years of training. Verdin's pattern glyphs are different from Ash Venn's. The differences are not errors. The differences are personal. "The marsh does not write the same pattern twice," Ash told him. "Your glyphs should not write the same pattern twice either. The variation is accuracy."

## Physical Media

### Obsidian Carving

The Summit Shrine's primary medium. Obsidian is carved with a tungsten-tipped stylus — Valdris forge product, one of the few tools manufactured specifically for writing. The carver must maintain consistent depth (2mm) and angle (approximately 45 degrees from vertical). Each name takes 20-40 minutes depending on length. The carver speaks the name aloud as they carve. The mountain requires the voice as well as the stone.

Obsidian plaques for household use are smaller, thinner, and carved with less ceremony. A family plaque is stored near the hearth and brought out on death anniversaries. The plaque is not read aloud — it is held. The holding is the reading.

### Reed-Paper

Mirithane's primary medium. Marsh reeds are pulped, pressed, and dried into thin sheets. The paper is rough, fibrous, and biodegradable. A reed-paper document left in water for a day dissolves. The impermanence is intentional. "Records should fade," the Water Temple's founding charter states. "Eternal records become laws. Laws become cages. The marsh does not cage."

Miri currency notes are a variant of reed-paper, infused with dormant *P. memoriae.* The bacteria absorb ambient latency and glow faintly for approximately one year of circulation. When the glow fades, the note is returned to the marsh for recharging. The annual recharging ritual is the closest Mirithane comes to a financial record. The record is not a number. The record is a light.

### Stenciled Steel

Glim unit IDs are applied to chassis plates with paint and a metal stencil. The paint is industrial-grade, designed for visibility on matte gray steel. White or yellow. The stencil is reusable. The stencil is never cleaned between units — the bleeding edges of each ID are slightly different. A factory-new Glim ID is crisp. An Arkos sanctuary Glim ID is crisp — Vorn keepers maintain them. A Valdris Glim ID is worn to near-illegibility by mining dust. A Verge Glim has no ID. The surface tells the story.

### Latency Crystals

The most advanced storage medium in Aurelia. A crystalline lattice tuned to consciousness-residue frequency. When a memory is extracted into a crystal, the lattice physically reconfigures to encode the memory's pattern. The crystal is readable by touch — contact with a living consciousness transfers the memory. A crystal can be replayed approximately twenty times before the lattice degrades and the memory becomes too faint to read.

Crystals store more than memories. Solara's research institutes use crystals for experimental data. The Glass Forum archives store certain sensitive records on crystals rather than in the display wall's accessible memory. The Memory Trader's inventory is entirely crystals — she has more stored memories than any other entity in the federation. She has never published an index. The index is in her head, or somewhere else, or nowhere. The Verge does not ask.

## Public Writing

### Council Notices

Solara's primary form of public text. Posted on the Lumen Plaza's notice wall — a pale stone tablet repurposed from pre-Collapse ruins. Notices are hand-lettered by a council scribe. The script is the formal Council Register — clear, legible, 26 letterforms with serif-like terminals. The content is administrative: trade regulations, energy credit adjustments, Ballot Night schedules.

Thren citizenship review results are never posted as a public notice. They are read aloud in the council chamber. The reading is performance. The posting would be documentation. Documentation cannot be walked back. The council does not post Thren citizenship results. This is policy. This is the policy that matters most.

### Guild Marks

Valdris's visible writing system. Every forge bears a guild mark — a symbol carved into the stone above the entrance. The mark indicates the forge's founding year, the founding smith's name, and the guild rank of the current master. Marks are read by guild members, not by the public. The public doesn't need to read them. The public knows which forge is which by the sound of its anvil in the evening cascade.

Guild marks are updated when a new master takes over. The old mark is not erased. It is carved over — a palimpsest of succession. The oldest forge in Valdris, Forge-1 at Basecamp, has a mark stone with seventeen layers of carving. The original layer is 180 years old. It is barely legible. The guild records say it was carved by Forge-Master Kael-1 — the first human Kael, the ancestor of Orin's line. The name is there, under sixteen layers of successors. The mountain remembers the names. The stone remembers the depth.

### The Memory Wall

The outer surface of the Memory Trader's stall — the salvaged door — is covered in objects people have left and taken. Some bear text: a fragment of obsidian with a name. A data crystal with a scratched label in Solara letterforms. A Vorn chassis plate with a designation glyph. The text is incidental. The wall is not meant to be read. The wall is meant to be seen, and touched, and added to, and removed from. The writing on the wall is the least important part of the wall. The wall is a surface. The surface is where things rest, briefly, before moving on.
