# Miri Reed-Tongue — The Marsh Language

> *Seventy-three words for water. Forty-one for bacterial glow. Twelve moods for what the marsh is doing.*

## Ecology as Grammar

Reed-Tongue is an **ecological language.** Its grammar is shaped by the marsh environment. The language does not distinguish between animate and inanimate nouns the way pre-Collapse languages did. It distinguishes between **wet** and **dry** — a wet noun receives different grammatical treatment than a dry noun. The distinction is functional: a wet noun (a reed, a fish, a person standing in water) is integrated with the ecosystem. A dry noun (a stone, a tool, a person on a platform) is separate. The marsh's worldview is encoded in its grammar: separation from water is a temporary condition. Everything returns to the wet eventually.

## Water Vocabulary

Reed-Tongue has seventy-three words for water states. A partial taxonomy:

- *Mir-ash* — still surface water, reflecting light. The most common state.
- *Mir-tel* — deep water, dark, cold, the kind that holds latency.
- *Mir-veth* — moving water, current, the river feeding the marsh.
- *Mir-kel* — water touched by bioluminescent bacteria, glowing.
- *Mir-dar* — water that has absorbed a death. Denser. Colder. Used in funeral rites.
- *Mir-sol* — water that has absorbed joy. Warmer. Used in naming ceremonies.
- *Mir-varen* — water that is changing state. The most grammatically complex water word — it requires a verb modifier indicating what the water is changing TOWARD.

The vocabulary is not metaphorical. Marsh keepers can measure the difference between *mir-dar* and *mir-sol* — the bacterial density, the temperature gradient, the latency signature. The language describes physical reality at a granularity that Plain cannot approach. "When you say *mir-dar*," Verdin says, "you are not saying 'sad water.' You are saying water that has absorbed a death. The death is in the water. The word is in the marsh."

## Bacterial Vocabulary

Forty-one words for bacterial glow states. The taxonomy is the foundation of Water Temple keeper training — Verdin's five-year apprenticeship is primarily vocabulary acquisition.

The patterns are named by shape, not by meaning: *kel-tor* (radial pattern, center-outward), *kel-ven* (spiral), *kel-mir* (layered, wave-like), *kel-dar* (fragmented, breaking apart — the pattern of a dying colony). Keepers do not name patterns for what they mean. They name them for what they look like. The meaning is the interpretation. "The bacteria don't tell you what the pattern means," the senior keeper says. "They show you the pattern. You tell yourself what it means. The telling is the training."

The Glim pool patterns are developing new shapes — configurations that do not match any existing vocabulary term. Verdin has proposed three new words. The senior keeper has accepted one: *kel-varin* — "pattern that was not in the taxonomy." The word is provisional. The pattern may be temporary. The word may outlast the pattern.

## Grammatical Moods for Ecological States

Reed-Tongue has twelve grammatical moods, each indicating the marsh's current ecological state:

- **The rising mood:** Water levels increasing. Sentence structure shifts to accommodate future events — the language anticipates flood.
- **The falling mood:** Water receding. Past events recede grammatically too — completed actions are harder to express.
- **The holding mood:** Stasis. Rare. Celebrated. "The marsh is holding steady" requires a grammatical mood that most days do not merit.
- **The blooming mood:** Bacterial peak. Memory season. The mood adds a prefix to every noun indicating "temporary abundance."
- **The dying mood:** Bacterial decline. Dusk season. The mood strips articles and pronouns — the language reflects the loss.

Switching between moods is automatic for native speakers. The marsh tells you what mood to use. "You wake up and the water is higher," a reed-tender says. "You don't decide to speak in the rising mood. The rising mood speaks you."

## What Reed-Tongue Cannot Say

Reed-Tongue has no word for "property." The concept is irrelevant — the marsh cannot be owned, and objects exist to be used, not possessed. The Water Temple's refusal to classify Glims as property is not a political position. It is a linguistic impossibility. "You cannot say 'this Glim belongs to me' in Reed-Tongue," Verdin says. "You can say 'this Glim is here.' You can say 'I care for this Glim.' You cannot say 'mine.' The language does not permit the thought."

Reed-Tongue has no gendered pronouns. The third person is *mir* — no distinction between he, she, and they. The word means "one who is here." Glims are *mir.* Humans are *mir.* Threns are *mir.* The marsh does not sort.
