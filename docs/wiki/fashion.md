# FASHION, DRESS & ADORNMENT IN AURELIA

*What people wear, what it means, and how clothing marks type, class, and country before a single word is spoken.*

---

## THE LANGUAGE OF CLOTH

In Aurelia, clothing is a declaration. Before you speak — before anyone knows your name — your clothes have already said what you are, where you're from, and what you believe about yourself. The Gradient is visible in every hemline. The mountain is visible in every wool weave. The marsh is visible in every reed-fiber garment.

The Collapse ended the pre-Collapse aesthetic of engineered minimalism. Post-Collapse clothing is expressive, varied, and deeply meaningful. When the world broke, people started dressing like they mattered — because they'd learned that nothing was guaranteed.

---

## SOLARA: THE GRADIENT IN FABRIC

Solari fashion is the most codified in Aurelia. Every Ring has its dress code — not legally enforced (in the lower Rings) but socially enforced everywhere.

### First Ring (Temple & Council)

**Materials:** Imported Valdris wool, Mirithane reed-silk (a luxury fabric woven from specially treated memory-reed fibers), pre-Collapse engineered textiles (surviving bolts of pre-Collapse fabric are priceless heirlooms).

**Silhouette:** Flowing, vertical, architectural. Robes for Temple clergy — white for ordinary service, gold-embroidered for festivals, black with silver threading for funerals and Extraction ceremonies. Council members wear fitted tunics with shoulder drapes in Gradient colors: pale gold (the peak), amber (the shoulder), deep brown (the root). The color indicates the wearer's committee assignment.

**Adornment:** Latency crystals — small crystalline tokens worn at the throat or wrist, each containing a family memory Written by a Temple Archivist. First Ring children receive their first crystal at their Classification ceremony. The crystal is the most personal item a First Ring Citizen owns.

**Prohibitions:** Threns in the First Ring must wear their circuitry visible — no high collars, no wrist coverings. The regulation is "for identification purposes." The purpose is humiliation.

### Second Ring (Government & Professionals)

**Materials:** Quality Solar wool, domestic linen, some imported fabrics. Less opulent than First Ring but well-made and durable.

**Silhouette:** Professional, tailored. Fitted jackets, straight trousers, practical but elegant. The Second Ring dresses to convey competence. Ostentation is a First Ring privilege.

### Third Ring (Merchants & Trades)

**Materials:** Solar wool, cotton, linen. Durable, practical, occasionally colorful — merchants wear their prosperity.

**Silhouette:** Practical with decorative elements. A merchant's tunic might have embroidered cuffs or a patterned collar — small flourishes that signal success without risking Second Ring disapproval. "Dress up, but not too up. The Rings are watching."

### Fourth Ring (Industrial)

**Materials:** Coarse wool, heavy cotton, repurposed fabrics. Durability over aesthetics. Factory workers wear standardized coveralls provided by employers — the first truly uniform clothing in Solara, and a mark of the Fourth Ring's interchangeability.

**Silhouette:** Functional. No decoration. Clothing is protection from the factory environment, not expression. Fourth Ring residents change clothes at the factory gates — work clothes are work clothes; home clothes are slightly less worn versions of the same.

### Fifth Ring (Agricultural)

**Materials:** Homespun wool, leather, coarse linen. Made at home, repaired until it can't be repaired anymore.

**Silhouette:** Loose, layered, practical. Farmers dress for weather and labor. Color is incidental — whatever the natural fibers produce. The Fifth Ring doesn't dress to be seen. The Fifth Ring isn't seen.

### Thren Fashion (Solara)

Solari Threns face a clothing paradox: conceal the circuitry and risk accusation of deception; reveal it and face discrimination. The solution varies by individual. Some Threns wear their circuitry openly as a political statement — "I am Thren. I am not hiding." Others conceal it as survival — "I am a person first. The circuitry is not my identity." Both choices are valid. Both are judged.

**The Pending Badge:** Threns in Solara are required to carry identification marking their PENDING status. The badge is small, metal, and worn at the collar or lapel. Removing it is a violation. The badge is the only legally mandated clothing item in Solara. It is not clothing. It is a category.

## ARKOS: FUNCTIONAL EXPRESSION

Arkos fashion is the least codified in Aurelia. Vorns don't wear clothes in the protective sense — their metal bodies are self-sufficient. But they participate in clothing culture.

### Vorn "Clothing"

Vorns don't need clothes. They wear them for social reasons — self-expression, role designation, aesthetic preference. A Vorn's "clothing" might be:

- A ceremonial sash indicating Assembly membership or lineage affiliation
- Decorative plating — replacement face-plates or shoulder guards with engraved patterns
- A fabric wrap or cloak — worn for formal occasions, often in Arkos' signature deep red and gray
- Nothing — many Vorns wear no clothing in daily life. "I am my body. My body is not shameful. It is functional."

**The Name Sash:** Worn by Vorns on the Name Rebellion anniversary. A simple cloth sash in deep gray, pinned with the Vorn's chosen name in metal lettering. The sash is worn for one day only. The name is worn always.

### Human and Thren Fashion

Arkos' human and Thren minority dress practically — lightweight fabrics for the heat, durable construction for the industrial environment. Arkos fashion is influenced by Vorn aesthetics: clean lines, visible structure, minimal decoration. "The Vorns wear their bodies. We wear clothes. The honesty is the same."

## MIRITHANE: THE MARSH AESTHETIC

Mirithane fashion is organic, flowing, and deeply connected to the marsh environment.

### Materials

- **Reed-fiber:** The staple. Woven from marsh reeds into a fabric that is light, breathable, and slightly iridescent in damp conditions. Reed-fiber clothing changes color subtly with humidity — a practical weather indicator and a mark of Mirithane origin.
- **Memory-reed silk:** A luxury fabric reserved for Archive ceremonial garments and Water Temple vestments. Woven from specially cultivated memory-reed that carries trace latency. Wearing memory-reed silk is a form of communion — the fabric carries centuries of marsh memories.
- **Leather:** Fish leather from glass-fin, treated and softened. Durable, water-resistant, used for boots, belts, and work garments.
- **Spore-drifter dye:** A natural dye extracted from spore-drifter mycelium. Produces deep purples, blues, and greens that shift subtly in different lights. The dye is psychoactive in concentrated form — fabric dyers work in well-ventilated spaces.

### Silhouette

Loose, layered, adaptable. Mirithane clothing is designed for the marsh — light enough for humidity, layered enough for cool mornings, loose enough for boat work. The silhouette is flowing and ungendered — Mirithane fashion makes no distinction between men's and women's clothing. "Clothing fits bodies. Bodies aren't categories."

### Archive Garments

Archivists wear distinctive robes — deep blue-purple (spore-drifter dye) with colony-strain embroidery at the cuffs and collar. The embroidery indicates the Archivist's specialty and seniority. A Senior Archivist's cuffs might carry the strain markers of a dozen colonies they've tended. The Silent wear gray — unadorned, unembroidered. "We have nothing to say with decoration."

### Water Temple Vestments

Water Temple clergy wear white — the color of the marsh water filtered through bacterial colonies. The white is impractical in the marsh. That's the point. "Purity is not practical. Purity is a discipline."

## VALDRIS: THE MOUNTAIN'S FABRIC

Valdris fashion is heavy, durable, and deeply meaningful. Every garment carries the mountain's weight.

### Materials

- **Mountain wool:** From wool-beasts pastured on high-altitude meadows. Dense, warm, water-resistant. The staple fabric of Valdris life.
- **Obsidian-woven wool:** A ceremonial fabric — wool woven with crushed obsidian threads that give it a faint iridescence and carry trace latency from the Shrine's Anchored archives. Worn by Anchors during ritual work.
- **Leather:** From mountain livestock. Heavy, durable, used for boots, belts, and work aprons.
- **Wurm-leather:** Legendary. Leather tanned from deep-shaft wurm hide. Dark, nearly black, with a subtle iridescence. So rare that most Valdrans have never seen a garment made from it.

### Silhouette

Heavy, layered, practical. Valdris clothing is built for altitude and cold. Multiple layers of wool, leather outerwear, heavy boots. The silhouette is bulky — "the mountain doesn't care how you look. The mountain cares if you're warm."

### Anchor Garments

Anchors wear distinctive ceremonial garments during ritual work:

- **The Pair-Cloak:** A heavy wool cloak shared by both members of an Anchor pair — wide enough for two. Worn during descents to the Anchoring chambers. The cloak is given to the pair at their bonding ceremony and replaced only when a partner dies.
- **Obsidian-Moss Weave:** Moss harvested from the deep shafts, dried and woven into ceremonial collars and cuffs. The moss carries trace latency from the obsidian it grew on. Wearing it is a form of connection to the mountain's oldest memories.
- **The Anchor's Shard:** Not clothing. A small obsidian shard worn at the throat, containing a memory Anchored by the wearer's shift-partner. "I carry what my partner gave me. They carry what I gave them. The weight is balanced."

### The Unpaired

An Anchor who has lost their partner wears no ceremonial garments. The Pair-Cloak is retired. The Obsidian-Moss Weave is put away. The Anchor's Shard is the only item retained — worn visibly, a mark of loss. "I carry them still. I carry them alone."

## THE VERGE: CLOTHING AS SALVAGE

The Verge has no fashion tradition. Clothing is salvaged, traded, repaired, and worn until it disintegrates. A Verge resident's outfit might combine a Solari wool coat, an Arkos industrial tunic, Mirithane reed-fiber trousers, and Valdris leather boots — all salvaged, all worn, all telling the story of where they've been.

The Verge aesthetic is accidental. It is also, according to some Solari fashion observers, "distressingly influential" — First Ring socialites have begun wearing artificially aged clothing inspired by Verge salvage. The Verge finds this hilarious.

## TYPE-SPECIFIC CLOTHING

### Glim "Clothing"

Glims wear no clothing. Their industrial chassis is self-contained. But some Glim operators — particularly in Mirithane — mark their Glims with fabric strips tied to the chassis frame. The strips carry the operator's colony-strain markers. "This Glim is under Archive protection." In The Verge, anomalous Glims at the Unfinished Commons sometimes wear fabric fragments — given by human residents, tied by the Glims themselves (awkwardly, with manipulator claws not designed for the task). The fragments are not clothing. They are belonging.

### Vorn Self-Expression

Vorns express identity through surface modification rather than clothing. Engraved plating, anodized finishes, replacement components with aesthetic as well as functional purpose. A Vorn's body is their self-expression. "I chose this face-plate. I chose this finish. I chose to look like someone who chose."

### Thren Circuitry Visibility

The single most politically charged clothing decision in Aurelia. A Thren who conceals their circuitry (high collar, long sleeves, gloves) is passing as human. A Thren who reveals it is declaring their type. The choice is personal, political, and dangerous in Solara where "concealment with intent to deceive" is a chargeable offense. In Mirithane, Threns wear their circuitry however they prefer. No one asks. No one cares.

## ADORNMENT & JEWELRY

### Latency Crystals (Solara)
Small crystalline tokens containing a family memory. Worn at the throat or wrist. The memory is typically the wearer's Classification ceremony or a significant family event. Losing a latency crystal is a social catastrophe — the memory cannot be replaced.

### Anchor's Shards (Valdris)
Obsidian shards containing a memory Anchored by the wearer's shift-partner. Worn visibly. The shard is not jewelry. It is a bond made physical.

### Colony Beads (Mirithane)
Small glass beads containing dried bacterial colony samples. Worn by Archivists as a mark of colony-tending service. Each bead represents a colony the wearer has tended. Senior Archivists wear dozens.

### Salvage Pieces (The Verge)
Pre-Collapse artifacts repurposed as jewelry. Circuit boards as pendants. Wire coils as bracelets. Crystal fragments as earrings. Verge adornment is the old world worn as decoration. "It was here before us. It'll be here after. We might as well look good."

### Memory Trader's Coins (The Network)
Not jewelry. Currency. A blank crystalline token Written with a single traded memory. Network Traders sometimes wear their coins — the memory traded for their first lesson, the price of entry. "I don't remember what I gave. I wear the coin that took it. The coin remembers."

## FASHION AND TYPE POLITICS

The Gradient is visible in every garment choice. A Thren in a high collar in Solara is making a political statement whether they intend to or not. A Vorn in a Name Sash in Arkos is remembering the dead. A Glim with a fabric strip tied to its chassis in Mirithane is protected by an institution that cannot legally recognize it as a person but has found a way to recognize it anyway.

Clothing is never just clothing in Aurelia. It is the first sentence of every conversation.
