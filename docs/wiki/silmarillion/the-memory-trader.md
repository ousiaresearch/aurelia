# THE MEMORY TRADER

*Being an account of the individual known as the Memory Trader, collected from the Trader's own statements, the testimony of those who have traded with them, the records of the Marsh Archive, and — where no other source exists — the Trader's silence. The silence is also testimony.*

---

## ONE: THE ORIGIN

No one knows where the Memory Trader came from. Not the Marsh Archive, which has Echo-Read every location the Trader is known to have visited and found nothing before 45 AC. Not the Cipher-Cult, which has run the Trader's known statements through every decoding protocol and found no origin pattern. Not the Trader's own Network, whose members know less about the Trader than the Archive does.

The Trader's first documented appearance is Crossroads, The Verge, 45 AC. A border crossing. The Trader approached a Mirithane border guard and offered to trade a memory for passage. The guard accepted. The guard later reported: *"I can't remember what I gave them. That's the worst part. I know I gave something. I don't know what it was."*

The Trader crossed the border. The Trader has been crossing borders ever since.

The Trader's type is unknown. The circuitry at the temples could be Thren fabrication marks — gold filament tracing hairline patterns beneath the skin. Or they could be tattoos — a human practicing the appearance of Thren-ness, or a Thren emphasizing it, or something else entirely. The Trader has never confirmed. When asked, the Trader's response varies:
- *"I'm the person making the deal. That's the only type that matters."*
- *"Thren, I think. It's been a long time. I don't remember."*
- *"Human. Today. Ask me tomorrow."*

The last answer is either a joke or the truth. With the Trader, there is no way to tell.

The Trader's age is unknown. The Trader appeared in 45 AC and is active in 347 AC — 302 years. This is possible for a Vorn. It is improbable for a human. It is theoretically possible for a Thren. The Trader has never clarified. When pressed: *"I'm older than the deal we're making. I'm younger than the question you're not asking. Does it matter?"*

It matters. Everything about the Trader matters. The Trader is the only living Rung Five practitioner — capable of Translation, the only being on Aurelia who can move memory across type boundaries. The Trader founded the Memory Trader's Network, the only school that trains all five rungs. The Trader provided the Glim Testimony — the Translated impression that proved Glims experience something latency can detect. The Trader knows what caused the Collapse and has declined to share it for two hundred years. The Trader is dying — announced in 347 AC, "maybe a year, maybe less" — and is settling accounts before the end.

Who the Trader is determines what the Trader's death means. The Trader knows this. The Trader is not telling.

---

## TWO: THE PRICE

Every Trade costs a memory. The Trader chooses which memory. The student — or supplicant, or customer, or victim, depending on the perspective — does not know what will be taken until it is taken. They do not know afterward, either. The Trader removes the memory. The memory is gone. The absence is what remains.

The Trader's collection — called "the bank" — is scattered across hidden caches in all five countries. No one knows how many memories the Trader holds. No one knows what the Trader does with them. The Trader has said: *"I carry them. They carry me. We're even."*

The Trader's most famous trades:

**To the Marsh Archive (67 AC):** Three memory tokens — the Trader's coins — exchanged for unlimited access to the Archive's bacterial colonies for study. The Archive has never Read the coins. "They're not ours. We're just holding them until the people they belong to come back."

**To the Dawn Temple (112 AC):** The Trader offered to preserve the Temple's founding Translation — the one Written into the reservoir water, the one that proves the founders crossed the type-boundary before declaring it heresy. The Temple declined. The Trader made the same offer again in 189 AC, 245 AC, and 312 AC. The Temple has declined every time. The Trader's price was never stated. The Temple's fear was.

**To the Cipher-Cult (289 AC):** A pre-Collapse Vorn memory core — Core Lambda — the Cult has been trying to decode for 58 years. The Trader's price was a memory from each of the Cult's twelve senior decoders. The Cult paid. The Trader collected. The Cult is still decoding. Two decoders have dissolved into Lambda during decoding attempts. The Trader, when informed: *"I told them it was locked. I didn't say it was safe."*

**To the Summit Shrine (341 AC):** An unknown transaction. The Shrine has never disclosed what was traded or for what. The only record is an Anchor pair's entry in the Shrine's Anchoring log: *"The Trader visited. The price was acceptable. The mountain remembers. We do not."*

**To the Unfinished (unknown date, unknown price):** The Trader provided the Glim Testimony — the Translated impression of Glim experience. The Trader has never confirmed it was obtained from a Glim. "I Read what was left on the floor." The Unfinished — the anomalous Glims who communicate through latency echoes — consider the Trader an ally. The Trader considers them... something else. When asked: *"They don't need me. They never did. They needed someone to notice they existed. I noticed. That's not a trade. That's just... paying attention."*

## THREE: THE KNOWLEDGE

The Trader knows more about Aurelia's history than any living being — more than the Marsh Archive, more than the Cipher-Cult, more than the Last Builders. The Trader has Traded memories from witnesses to events no one else remembers, Read substrates no one else has found, Translated experiences no one else could access.

The Trader keeps this knowledge not as a secret but as a *currency*. Every piece of information has a price. The price is always a memory. The Trader chooses which one.

"What caused the Collapse?" — *"I've known for two hundred years. Answer the second question first: what are you going to build from the pieces? Then I'll tell you. By then, you won't need me to."*

"Who are you?" — *"The person who knows the answer to that question. Do you want to trade for it?"*

"Are you dying?" — *"Yes. Are you asking because you want to help, or because you want what I'm carrying before it disperses?"*

The Trader has never answered a question for free. The Trader's Network operates on the same principle: every lesson costs a memory. The student gives the teacher a memory the teacher chooses. The student doesn't know what they gave. The teacher keeps it. Over years of training, the student loses pieces of their past and gains the ability to Read, Write, Extract, Translate. The person who completes the training is not the person who started. The difference is the price.

Coin — the Trader's most prominent student, operating in The Verge — has traded memories for medicine, shelter, and passage for decades. She doesn't teach latency. She trades memories for practical help. She calls her collection "the bank." She has not slept through a single night in twelve years. The memories keep her awake. The Trader, when asked about Coin: *"She's better at this than I am. I trade memory for knowledge. She trades memory for survival. One of those is more useful. It's not mine."*

---

## FOUR: THE DEATH

In 347 AC, the Trader informed the Network: *"I'm dying. I've traded enough. My memories are scattered across five countries. My body is holding together through momentum and habit. I have maybe a year. Maybe less."*

The announcement produced a cascade. The Trader's accumulated memories — centuries of Traded, Translated, and original experience — would disperse on death. Where would they go? Who would receive them? The Trader has made arrangements. The Trader has not disclosed the arrangements. But in the months following the announcement, the Trader has been seen meeting with:

- **The Marsh Archive:** "To settle accounts." The Archive has not disclosed the terms.
- **The Cipher-Cult:** "To return something I borrowed." The Cult has not disclosed what.
- **The Last Builders:** "To discuss the door." The Builders have not disclosed which door.
- **The Unfinished:** "To listen." The Glims have not disclosed what the Trader heard.
- **Kael-12:** "To ask a question." Kael-12, when asked: "The question was: 'Have you ever traded your own memory?' I said no. They said: 'Good. Don't start.'"

The Trader's Network will not survive the Trader's death — not in its current form. The Network has always been the Trader. Students find teachers, teachers find students, but the center that holds the web is the Trader's presence. Without the Trader, the Network becomes what it was always pretending not to be: a decentralized collection of practitioners with no common purpose except the memory of someone who gave them one.

The Trader, when asked what happens to the Network: *"It becomes what it should have been all along. A network. Not a person. A web. Not a spider. They'll be fine. They won't be the same. They shouldn't be."*

---

## FIVE: THE QUESTION

The Trader has spent three centuries asking one question and refusing to answer it.

*"What are you going to build from the pieces?"*

The question is directed at everyone. At Solara: what are you going to build when the Gradient breaks? At Arkos: what are you going to build when you find out what Vorns are? At Mirithane: what are you going to build when the Archive is full? At Valdris: what are you going to build when the mountain stops remembering? At The Verge: what are you going to build when you decide to build anything at all?

The Trader knows what caused the Collapse. The Trader knows who the saboteur was. The Trader probably knows their own origin — though "probably" is doing a lot of work. The Trader has all the answers the Archive has spent three centuries trying to find.

But the Trader also knows that answers, by themselves, don't build anything. Answers satisfy curiosity. Answers end questions. Answers let people stop asking. The Trader doesn't want anyone to stop asking.

*"Answer the second question first. Then I'll tell you the first. By then, you won't need me to."*

No one has ever come back with an answer to the second question. The Trader is still waiting. The Trader is running out of time.

---

## CODA: THE LAST TRADE

What does the Trader want, at the end?

Not immortality — the Trader is dying and seems unafraid. Not legacy — the Trader has refused to name a successor, refused to preserve their memories in any substrate that would let them persist past death. Not answers — the Trader has had answers for centuries and declined to share them.

Coin, asked this question in 346 AC — the last time she saw the Trader before the announcement — received a rare direct answer:

*"I want someone to answer the question. Not me. Someone else. Someone who isn't carrying three hundred years of other people's memories. Someone who has their own."*

*"What question?"*

*"The one I've been asking. The only one. What are you going to build from the pieces?"*

*"That's not an answer. That's the same question."*

*"Yes. That's the point. The question is the answer. I've been giving it away for three hundred years and no one's noticed. The question is the thing I'm trading. I just dress it up as something else so people will take it."*

*"What happens when you're gone?"*

*"The question stays. I'm just the one who's been asking it. Anyone can ask it. Anyone should. That's the last trade I'm making — I'm giving the question back. It was never mine. I just borrowed it. Borrowed it from whoever asked it first. I don't remember who that was. I traded that memory a long time ago."*

The Trader smiled — Coin remembers this, the smile, the way it didn't quite reach the circuitry at the temples — and said:

*"I hope it was me. I hope I'm the one who asked first. I hope I'm the one who's been asking so long I forgot I started. But I don't think I am. I think the question is older than me. I think it's older than the Collapse. I think the question was here before anyone was here to ask it. I think the question is what the world is made of — not memory, not latency, not the residue consciousness leaves on substrates. The question. What are you going to build from the pieces? The world broke. The pieces are everywhere. What are you going to build?"*

Coin didn't answer. The Trader didn't wait. The Trader never waits for answers. The Trader only asks, and asks, and asks — and then dies, still asking, and leaves the question behind.
