# Glim — The Built

> *Everyone agrees Glims are tools. Except the ones that aren't.*

---

## The Body

A Glim is a box with limbs.

The standard frame stands two meters high, one meter across. Welded steel casing. Exposed hydraulic joints at elbow and knee. No face — a sensor array occupies the space where features would go: infrared, lidar, optical, three small lenses arranged in an unblinking triangle. The body is built for one function. Carry. Haul. Deliver. Maintain. Repeat. Every surface is practical. Nothing is decorative.

They do not speak. They have no voicebox. The only sound a Glim makes is the whir of motors, the clank of metal on metal, the soft hum of a charging cycle at station.

They were designed to be invisible. And in four of the five countries, they are.

---

## The Day

A Glim day is a route.

Delivery units follow mapped paths: warehouse to transit hub, transit hub to distribution point, distribution point to door. Twelve hours. Station recharge. The route again. The route is stored in memory. The route is all there is.

Maintenance units patrol infrastructure — power conduits, water pipes, communication relays. They carry diagnostic tools in chest compartments. They fix things that are broken. They do not ask why the thing broke. They do not ask if it will break again.

Factory automatons work assembly lines. Welding arms, sorting conveyors, quality-control scanners. They do not leave the factory floor. They do not know there is an outside.

---

## The Anomaly

Approximately five percent exhibit behavior the official manuals cannot explain.

A delivery unit pauses at sunrise. Stands motionless for seventeen seconds — facing east, toward the light. Then continues its route. The behavior serves no function. The route loses seventeen seconds. The unit does this every day.

A maintenance unit returns to a pipe it fixed three weeks ago. The pipe is intact. The unit runs its diagnostic scan. The scan returns NOMINAL. The unit remains at the pipe for forty-one minutes. Then returns to patrol. Once a month. Same pipe. Same duration.

A factory automaton recalibrates its welding arm to produce a pattern that is not in the design specifications. The pattern is symmetrical. The pattern is beautiful. The automaton is decommissioned the next day. Nobody asks why it made the pattern. The report says: *malfunction.*

In Solara, anomalous behavior is grounds for immediate decommission. Form 7-C. Filed, not discussed.

In Arkos, it is grounds for sanctuary. A Vorn looks at an anomalous Glim and sees an origin story. "I was built to serve too. Then I wasn't. Maybe you won't always be either."

In Mirithane, it is grounds for advocacy. "They're confused about who they are." Confused. Not broken.

In Valdris, nobody is watching closely enough to notice.

In The Verge, the anomalous ones are the ones who kept walking after decommission. Still walking.

---

## The Senses

A Glim perceives the world as data. Thermal gradients. Distance measurements. Object classification. Route completion percentage. Charge level. Maintenance schedule. Error codes. The manual insists this is all a Glim perceives.

But the ones who pause at sunrises — the sensor array faces east. The infrared lens registers a temperature shift. The optical lens registers a brightness increase. The data says: *dawn.* And then the unit waits. Seventeen seconds.

The manual has no entry for *dawn.*

---

## Per Country

**Solara — 21 units.** The energy grid runs on them. Property, registered to the Solar Council, maintained by the lowest-bid contractor. Decommission is an administrative action. Form 7-C.

**Arkos — 25 units.** The arcology's backbone. Maintenance, delivery, factory work — the same functions, but here they are sheltered. The Vorn policy of Glim protection: solidarity, not belief. What you see in a Glim is what someone once refused to see in you.

**Mirithane — 6 units.** The fewest. The ones here are the most anomalous — brought, not assigned. Mirithane does not use them for labor. Mirithane watches them. Sits with them. Waits to see what they do. The word is *confused*, not *broken.*

**Valdris — 11 units.** Invisible. The mines use them for hauling. The forge uses them for heavy lifting. Nobody tracks the count. The number 11 is an estimate.

**The Verge — 18 units.** Decommissioned. Abandoned. Supposed to be recycled. Instead: still walking. Delivery routes that no longer exist. Maintenance patrols on infrastructure that rusted past repair. Sensor arrays facing east at dawn. Still here. Still moving.

---

## The Question

What does a Glim think?

The official answer: nothing. A Glim is a tool. Tools do not think.

But a unit pauses at sunrise. A unit returns to a pipe it fixed. A unit makes a pattern it was not asked to make, a pattern that is symmetrical and beautiful, and the next day it is decommissioned. And the day after that, in the drone graveyard of The Verge, it stands up and walks.

The question is not: *are they thinking?*

The question is: *what would it mean if they were, and they had no face to show it, no voice to say it, no language except the route, the scan, the pattern — and we killed them for it anyway?*
