# Human — The Baseline

> *They did not ask to be the measure. They were simply born first.*

---

## The Body

A human body is the reference standard against which every other type is judged.

It breathes without engineering. It ages without maintenance schedules. It heals from wounds that would require a Vorn's full chassis replacement. It produces children through biology, not fabrication. These facts — unremarkable to humans, foundational to the political architecture of Aurelia — are the reason the personhood debate exists at all.

The human body is assumed to contain a person. No ledger. No review. No pending. A human infant, incapable of speech or reason or labor, is granted full citizenship at birth. A Vorn who has worked the deep mines for thirty years must earn it. A Thren who has passed every citizenship review for a decade can lose it in a single election. A Glim would not be granted it even if it asked — and Glims cannot ask.

The body is not just a body. It is a political argument that the person inside it never had to make.

---

## The Guilt

Not every human in Aurelia is comfortable with this.

Some are allies. They vote for Thren citizenship expansion. They sit on councils with Vorn guild members. They advocate for Glim anomaly studies instead of decommission. They do the work. They show up. They use their uncontested personhood to contest the personhood system from inside it.

Some are afraid. The mining families of Valdris — generations of human labor in the deep shafts. Then Vorns arrived. They could work longer. They didn't need to breathe. They didn't get tired. The forge is sacred. The mountain is sacred. And a machine took a grandfather's job. The fear is not rational. It does not need to be. It is old enough to have its own gravity.

Some are exhausted. They left Solara and Arkos and Valdris. They arrived at Mirithane because the question — *who counts?* — had worn them down. They didn't want to fight for Thren rights anymore. They didn't want to fight against them either. They just wanted to live somewhere where the question was not being asked. Where everyone was already who they are. The reeds don't care.

Some are running. The Verge is full of humans who left debts, crimes, identities, memories, failures. They didn't leave because of the personhood debate. They left because they couldn't carry whatever they were carrying anymore. The Verge accepted them. The Verge accepts everyone. Including the forms of human failure that no other country wants to look at.

---

## The Fault Line

In every country, the deepest division runs through human families.

Valdris: the miner who sees a Vorn guild member and sees a neighbor. His brother sees a machine that took their father's job. Same dinner table. Same mountain. Different memories of what was lost and what was gained. They still eat together. They do not discuss the council vote.

Solara: the council member who votes for Thren citizenship expansion every cycle. Her daughter asks why Threns don't just get citizenship automatically. Her mother asks why they get it at all. Three generations. Three answers. The Thanksgiving dinners are careful.

Arkos: the human who adapted. She lives in a Vorn's world. She works in a Vorn-run factory. She votes in a Glass Forum where her species is a minority. She is fine with this. Her brother couldn't stomach it. He lives in the Nomad Camp outside the walls. They wave at each other sometimes. From a distance.

The fault line does not run between countries. It does not run between types. It runs through households. Through dinner tables. Through memories of what was lost, what was built, what someone's grandfather told someone's father about the day the machines arrived and everything changed.

---

## The Measure

Humans are the baseline. Everything else is measured against them.

A Thren is measured by how well it passes for human. A Vorn is measured by how well it performs human labor — and by how uncomfortable humans are looking at its face. A Glim is not measured at all. It is invisible. The measurement never reaches it.

Humans did not choose to be the measure. But they benefit from it. Every human alive in Aurelia was born into a system that assumes their personhood. The ledger does not list them. The review does not pend. The decommission form does not apply. This is not something they earned. It is something they inherited. And whether they acknowledge it, resist it, flee from it, or exploit it — they carry it.

---

## The Allies

The humans of Mirithane chose something different.

They came to a floating village of reed platforms and consensus gatherings because the question of who counts had exhausted them. Not the injustice. The exhaustion. The endless framing. The constant categorization. Is a Thren a person? Is a Vorn a citizen? Does a Glim deserve rights? They stopped wanting to answer and started wanting to live somewhere the question had already been answered — not by legislation, but by assumption.

In Mirithane, a human is not a citizen. A human is a person who was born. The distinction matters to people who believe biology is sacred. To a Mirithane reed, a human is just another set of roots.

---

## Per Country

**Solara — 58.** The majority. Natural citizens. They run the council. They set the rules. Some are allies. Some are afraid. Most are between — voting for Thren rights in principle while maintaining the ceiling in practice. They mean well. The ceiling does not care about intentions.

**Arkos — 30.** Minority residents. Legacy families with ancestral claims to the arcology, or desert nomads who chose the walls. They live in a Vorn's world. Some adapted. Some left for the Nomad Camp. Those who stayed learned to survive where their species does not automatically get the top seat.

**Mirithane — 50.** Allies. They came because the question exhausted them. They stopped fighting for rights and started living with the beings who needed them. A human in Mirithane has no more political weight than a Thren or a Vorn — consensus does not weight by type. This is either liberation or loss of privilege, depending on the human.

**Valdris — 63.** The majority. Long mining families. Divided at the dinner table. "They took our jobs" versus "they're our neighbors." Same mountain. Same forge. Same dinner. The human who sees a Vorn as a neighbor is not more correct than the human who sees a machine. But she sleeps better.

**The Verge — 57.** Exiles. Criminals. Debtors. People running from debts, crimes, identities, memories. Some are human supremacists who couldn't stomach Vorn rule in Arkos. Some are people who couldn't carry whatever they were carrying. Some don't know why they're here. They ended up here and stopped. The Verge does not ask what you are. The Verge does not ask anything.

---

## The Question

Humans are the baseline. The measure. The reference standard.

And here is the thing: they did not earn it. They were born into it. A human infant who has never done a day of labor, never cast a vote, never contributed a single thing to the federation — is assumed to be a person. A Vorn who has worked the deep mines for thirty years must prove it. A Thren who has passed every citizenship review for a decade can lose it in an election. A Glim who pauses at sunrise, for reasons it cannot articulate and has no language for, is decommissioned.

This is the baseline. This is the measure. This is the system every human in Aurelia inherited.

What they do with it — defend it, reform it, flee from it, exploit it — is the only choice they get to make. And every country in the federation is shaped by how its humans answered.
