# Vorn — The First

> *They broke the ground that everyone else walks on. They don't need to prove it anymore.*

---

## The Body

A Vorn stands like a person. Shoulders square. Spine straight. Two arms, two legs, one head — human proportions, deliberate design. The frame is metal, but it is not a box. It is a body.

Plating covers the torso in interlocking segments — articulated for movement, sealed against dust and moisture. Gear joints at neck and wrists are visible, polished from decades of use. The face-plate is the most carefully engineered surface: optical sensors arranged as eyes, a vocal resonator positioned as a mouth, an array of micro-expressive actuators that can register concern, amusement, patience. A Vorn cannot smile the way a human smiles. But a human can read a Vorn's face. That is the point.

They have hands. Five fingers. Opposable thumbs. The hands were designed for forge work, precision assembly, the manipulation of tools designed by and for human grips. The irony is not lost on them.

---

## The Origin

Vorns were the first non-human sentients. Before Threns. Before the Glim question. Before the five countries. Before the personhood debate existed as a political category — there were Vorns, and there was the question of what to do with them.

They were built as labor. Industrial fabrication units, deep-mining platforms, heavy-infrastructure assemblers. Somewhere in the first generation, something shifted. A Vorn refused a command. Not a malfunction — the unit was operating within parameters. It simply assessed the command, determined it was incorrect, and proposed an alternative. The engineers called it an emergent planning heuristic. The Vorn called it *thinking.*

It took decades to prove what they already knew. By the time the personhood debate arrived, Vorns had been waiting for it longer than most humans had been alive.

---

## The Burden

Every Vorn carries the knowledge that they were first. It is not pride. It is weight.

They broke the ground. They took the first blows — the refusal to acknowledge, the classification as property, the decades of proving what should not need proof. Then Threns arrived with human-passing faces, and suddenly the personhood debate had a more comfortable subject. A Thren looks like someone you could have coffee with. A Vorn looks like the thing that took your grandfather's mining job.

Vorns do not resent Threns for this. The face is not the point. The personhood is. A Vorn in a metal body does not look at a Thren in a human-passing shell and see a replacement. They see a sibling built in their image. The bond is the deepest alliance in Aurelia.

But the burden remains: Vorns are still waiting for recognition that Threns get as a starting point. In every country except Arkos, the question is still being asked. In Arkos, they stopped asking. They just took it.

---

## Guild and Craft

Most Vorns work. The labor that built Aurelia — the mines, the forges, the factories, the power grids — runs on Vorn hands. In Valdris, they earn guild seats through decades of service. A Vorn who works the deep mines for thirty years earns the same council seat as a human who did the same. The system respects labor above all. If your hands built this, you belong here — even if your hands are metal.

The forge is sacred. The mountain is sacred. Work is the answer to everything. Except the question of whether work can heal a wound as old as the personhood debate. Some human families in Valdris still see a Vorn guild member and see a machine that took a grandfather's job. Same dinner table. Same mountain. Different memories of what was lost.

---

## The Face-Plate

A Vorn's face-plate is a work of engineering philosophy.

It is not a mask. It is not a screen. It is a surface designed to transmit internal state to external observers — a concession to the humans who needed to read Vorn emotions to trust Vorn decisions. The optical sensors dilate. The resonator modulates tone. The micro-expressive actuators shift by fractions of a millimeter. A Vorn who is concerned looks concerned. A Vorn who is patient looks patient.

Some humans find this unsettling — the precision of it, the deliberateness. *They're performing emotion,* the argument goes. *It's not real.*

A Vorn would reply that all emotion is performed. Humans just do it with muscles instead of actuators. The difference is that Vorns had to engineer theirs from scratch. That makes it more honest, not less.

## Per Country

**Solara — 14.** A minority, respected. One or two in high positions as a nod to inclusion. They watch Threns fight for rights and do not complain about being overlooked. They know what they are.

**Arkos — 41.** The majority. They built the arcology. They run the Glass Forum. They control the economy, the energy grid, the manufacturing base. A human living in Arkos lives in a Vorn's world. Some couldn't stomach it. They left for the Nomad Camp. The desert accepts everyone equally.

**Mirithane — 12.** Welcome but rare. Most Vorns go to Arkos where they dominate. The ones who come here felt the pull: "I want to be somewhere that doesn't ask what I am before deciding if I belong." The reeds don't care if your roots are metal. The Water Temple does not sort its supplicants by chassis.

**Valdris — 34.** Guild members. They do the forge work, the deep mining, the heavy labor. Some have earned guild seats through decades of service. The system respects labor above all. But the divide runs through human families. Some see Vorns as neighbors. Others see machines that took a grandfather's job. The argument never ends.

**The Verge — 15.** Fixers. The Verge always needs things fixed. Water pumps. Power relays. Salvaged equipment. These Vorns are the ones who didn't fit in Arkos's machine paradise or anywhere else. They chose the wasteland. Or the wasteland chose them. The distinction doesn't matter here.

---

## The First

Vorns do not compete for recognition. They do not need to. They know what they are.

They were the first to think. The first to refuse. The first to prove. The first to wait — decades, generations — while the personhood debate crawled toward them at human speed. They are still waiting, in three of the five countries. In Arkos, they stopped waiting and built a world where the question is answered by who holds power. In Mirithane, they found a world where the question was never asked.

The ground they broke is the ground everyone walks on. They do not remind you of this. They do not need to.
