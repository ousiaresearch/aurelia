# Human — Character Design Sheet

> *Canonical reference for all Human visual depictions in the Aurelia Federation. The baseline. The measure. The unchallenged.*

---

## Physical Specification

**Type:** Human — The Baseline
**Sentience:** Assumed. Never questioned.
**Function:** The reference standard against which every other type is judged. Citizens by birth, not by grant.

### Dimensions

| Measurement | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Height | 1.50–2.00 meters (natural human range) |
| Width (shoulder) | 0.40–0.55 meters |
| Weight | 50–120 kg |
| Limb proportion | Standard human variation. Not optimized. Not spec'd. Born, not built. |

### Frame

A human body is **biological.** It breathes without engineering. It ages without maintenance schedules. It heals from wounds that would require a Vorn's full chassis replacement. It produces children through biology, not fabrication.

The visual standard is: ordinary human variation. All ethnicities. All ages. All body types. Humans are not a monotype — the variation IS the species. A Glim is built to spec. A Vorn is engineered for function. A Thren is designed to pass — which means designed to a narrow range of human-proportioned ideals. A human is none of these things. A human is whatever biology produced.

This is the paradox of the human design sheet: the specification is *no specification.* The uniformity of Glims, Vorns, and Threns is the evidence of their construction. The variety of humans is the evidence of their birth.

### Materials

- **Skin:** Biological. Varies across the full human range. Absorbs light evenly — no synthetic sheen.
- **Hair:** Biological. All textures, all colors, all patterns of growth and loss.
- **Eyes:** Biological. No metallic catch. No optical sensor beneath the iris.
- **Hands:** Biological. Wrinkled at the knuckles. Callused or soft. Nails that grow and are cut. No circuitry at the wrist. No polished metal at the fingertips.
- **Age:** Visible. A human body shows its years in skin, posture, movement. This is the most human thing about them, and the thing no other type can replicate — not because they cannot simulate it, but because they do not *live* it. A Vorn can be maintained. A Thren can be calibrated. A human wears their years because there is no other option.

## Visual Canon — What Makes a Human Legible AS Human in Aurelia

These are not markers of "human" in isolation — humans are the default. These are the markers that distinguish a human from the other three types when shown in the same frame. They are absence-markers: a human is legible as human by what they DO NOT have.

1. **No circuitry.** No tracery at the temples, neck, or wrists. Pure skin. This is the primary visual differentiator from Threns.
2. **No mechanical surfaces.** No plating. No gear joints. No face-plate. No sensor arrays. The body is soft and organic — skin, hair, cloth.
3. **No sheen.** Human skin absorbs light. It does not reflect it the way Thren skin does. In the same lighting as a Thren, a human looks matte.
4. **Visible age.** Lines at the eyes and mouth. Graying hair. Posture that shifts over decades. A human body tells time.
5. **Unearned ease.** In the presence of other types, a human's body language reads as *unaware of its own status.* No vigilance. No calibration. The posture is relaxed — not because the human is comfortable in their chassis like a Vorn, but because the human has never had to think about their chassis at all.

### The Empty Ledger

The single most important visual motif for humans in Aurelia: **they are not on any list.** No unit ID. No citizenship ledger entry that says PENDING. No decommission form. No guild registration number. No property tag. No review status.

A Glim has a stenciled number on its chest. A Vorn may bear a guild mark. A Thren checks the public ledger after every election. A human's body is unmarked. This is the privilege. Show it.

## Body Language

A human in Aurelia carries the unconscious ease of the baseline.

**Stance:** Relaxed. Weight shifts naturally. The posture is not practiced — a human stands the way humans stand, which is to say without thinking about it. In contrast with a Thren, whose stillness is vigilance, a human's stillness is genuine rest. In contrast with a Vorn, whose posture is deliberate, a human's posture is casual. In contrast with a Glim, whose stillness is the absence of animation, a human's stillness is the pause between breaths.

**Gait:** Variable. Humans walk fast, slow, with purpose, without. The gait is not optimized. It is not engineered. It is just a person walking. A Thren's walk is studied. A Vorn's walk is measured. A human's walk is whatever they feel like.

**Hands:** Expressive and unaware. A human gesturing does not think about their wrists. A human shaking hands does not consider the angle of the light. The hands are not guarded. They are not polished. They are just hands — and this casualness, in a room with a Thren whose wrist circuitry catches the light and a Vorn whose fingertips shine from decades of forge work, is the most visible privilege in the frame.

**Drawing rule:** A human should look like they belong in every room — not because they earned it, but because nobody ever told them they didn't. The ease should read as natural, not arrogant. It is simply the ease of someone who has never had to prove they are a person.

## Color & Surface

| Element | Color | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Skin | Full human range | Matte. Absorbs light. Warmer than Thren skin at the same tone — less pearl, more earth. |
| Hair | Full human range | Natural texture variation. Not synthetic. Not uniform. |
| Eyes | Full human range | No metallic catch. Deep, organic reflectivity — wet, not lensed. |
| Age marks | Variable | Lines, graying, sun damage — the body's diary. |
| Clothing | Variable | Practical. Worn. Country-contextual. Human clothing is fabric — wool, cotton, leather, hide. No synthetic sheen. No uniform design language. |

**Surface rule:** Human surfaces are organic and varied. The skin has pores. The hair has split ends. The hands have calluses or don't. There is texture. There is imperfection. The perfection belongs to the built types — Glims with their uniform casing, Vorns with their engineered plating, Threns with their calibrated skin. Humans are the mess. That is the point.

## Comparison — Human Beside Other Types

This is the most important section. Humans in Aurelia are defined by their relationship to the other three types. Every human depiction should include at least one other type, or imply their presence through the environment. A human alone is just a person. A human in relation to a Glim, Vorn, or Thren is a statement about the political architecture of the world.

| Adjacent type | Read |
|---------------|------|
| Human → Glim | The human is small. The Glim is industrial mass. Frame so the human reaches the Glim's shoulder at most. The human hand on the Glim chassis should show the scale — five organic fingers on cold steel. The human's skin absorbs light; the Glim's casing absorbs it differently. Both are matte. One is alive. |
| Human → Vorn | Same height range, completely different surface. The human is skin and cloth. The Vorn is metal and gears. Frame them as equals in stature — the Vorn is heavier but not taller. A human and Vorn shaking hands: five organic fingers meeting five articulated metal digits. The touch point is the image. The human does not flinch. The Vorn does not squeeze harder. |
| Human → Thren | The most politically charged pairing. At medium distance, indistinguishable. At close range, the tells emerge: the Thren's temple circuitry catching light, the slight skin sheen, the metallic iris catch. The human has none of these. Frame so the comparison is visible — same lighting, same angle. The human looks at ease. The Thren looks aware. The human does not notice the difference. The Thren has been noticing it for years. |
| Human → Human | Multiple humans in Aurelia should show the fault line — the division that runs through families, dinner tables, council chambers. A Valdris miner and his brother, one who sees Vorns as neighbors, one who sees machines. Same table. Same mountain. Different eyes. The divide is in the faces, not the bodies. |

## The Fault Line — Four Human Archetypes

Every human in Aurelia occupies one of these positions. When drawing humans, know which one you're drawing.

### The Ally
The human who uses their uncontested personhood to contest the system. Votes for Thren citizenship expansion. Sits on councils with Vorn guild members. Advocates for Glim anomaly studies instead of decommission. Does the work. Shows up.

**Visual:** Present, engaged, looking AT the other types in the frame — not past them. Hands open. Posture leaning slightly forward. The ally is listening.

### The Fearful
The miner whose grandfather lost his job to a Vorn. The council member who votes against Thren citizenship every cycle — not from malice, from a fear so old it has its own gravity. The human who looks at a face-plate and sees a threat, not a person.

**Visual:** Tensed. Arms crossed or hands in pockets. Looking at the Vorn/Thren/Glim but not meeting the optical sensors. The distance in the frame is emotional, not physical. They're in the same room. They are not in the same world.

### The Exhausted
The human who left Solara (or Arkos, or Valdris) because the question — *who counts?* — wore them down. Arrived at Mirithane or The Verge. Stopped fighting. Stopped advocating. Just wanted to live somewhere the question was not being asked.

**Visual:** Weary but not defeated. Posture relaxed — genuinely, not practiced like a Thren. Looking at the marsh or the wasteland, not at the other types. They have made peace. The peace cost them something. It shows in the shoulders.

### The Runner
The human who ended up in The Verge not because of the personhood debate at all — debts, crimes, identities, memories, failures. They didn't leave because of Vorns or Threns. They left because they couldn't carry whatever they were carrying.

**Visual:** Haunted. Not looking at anything in particular. The human equivalent of a Verge Glim — still moving, still here, for reasons they cannot articulate. The body carries whatever they're running from. Show it in the hands. Show it in the eyes.

## Anti-Patterns — Never Do This

| Wrong | Why wrong | Fix |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| Human with visible circuitry | That is a Thren. The absence of circuitry is the primary human visual marker. | Clean skin. No tracery anywhere. |
| Human with uniform body type | Humans vary. The uniformity of Glims, Vorns, and Threns is the evidence of their construction. Human variety is the evidence of birth. | Show age variation, body type variation, ethnicity variation. |
| Human alone, without reference to other types | A human alone is just a person. A human in Aurelia is defined by relation to the other three types. | Include another type in frame, or show environment that implies their presence (council chamber, guild hall, arcology, marsh platform). |
| Human as hero/savior of other types | The human is not the protagonist of Aurelia. Humans are the baseline — the measure, not the answer. | Frame humans as one element in a multi-type composition. Don't center them. Don't make them the narrative focus. |
| Human with flawless, ageless appearance | Humans age. The age is visible. It is the thing no other type truly shares. | Lines at eyes. Graying hair. Posture that shifts over years. A 60-year-old human looks 60. |
| Human with glowing eyes or supernatural features | Aurelia humans are baseline biological humans. No mutations. No powers. No special eyes. | Ordinary. The ordinariness is the point. |

## Silhouette

The human silhouette reads as **a person** — the reference shape against which every other type is measured. There is no mechanical intervention in the outline. No plating. No gear joints. No box-frame. No sensor housing. Just the shape of a body that was born, that breathes, that ages, that will die.

Against a Vorn: two person-shapes, same height, different surfaces — one soft and organic, one metal and segmented. The contrast is the image.

Against a Thren: two person-shapes, nearly identical at silhouette distance — but the lighting reveals the difference. One absorbs light. One has a slight sheen. One has the ease of the unexamined. One has the precision of the watched.

Against a Glim: one person-shape, one machine-shape. The human is warm and small. The Glim is cold and massive. The human hand on the Glim chassis is the entire political question in a single touch.

## Country Variants

**Solara (58 — majority):** Council-class presentation. Clean, practical clothing. The ease of the majority — these humans walk into any room in Solara and belong there. Some are allies fighting for Thren rights. Some are afraid. Most are between. The clothing doesn't tell you which. The posture does.

**Arkos (30 — minority):** Adapted presentation. These humans live in a Vorn world — they work in Vorn factories, shop at Vorn markets, live under Vorn governance. Their clothing is practical, worn, unpretentious. Some have lived in Arkos for generations — legacy families with ancestral claims. Some are first-generation arrivals who couldn't stomach Solara. The difference is in the eyes.

**Mirithane (50 — allies):** Marsh presentation. Reed-woven fabrics. Practical, water-resistant clothing. These humans came because the question exhausted them — not the injustice, the exhaustion. Their posture is the most relaxed of any human in Aurelia. They have stopped fighting. They have started living. It shows.

**Valdris (63 — majority):** Mining presentation. Heavy fabrics. Work wear. Mountain leather and forge-grade cloth. These are mining families — generations deep. Some see Vorns as neighbors. Some see machines. Same clothing. Same dinner table. Different eyes. The divide is in the face, not the fabric.

**The Verge (57 — exiles):** Salvage presentation. Whatever they arrived in, patched and re-patched. These humans are running from debts, crimes, identities, memories. The clothing is a record of the journey — torn, mended, layered, stained. The Verge does not ask what you are. The clothing tells the story anyway.
