# Literature & Storytelling in Aurelia

> *Every country tells stories. Every story is a position on the question.*
>

## The Oral Tradition

Writing survived the Collapse in fragments. Books — physical books — are rare, salvaged from pre-Collapse ruins and preserved in Solara's council archives. Most Aurelians cannot read. Literacy is concentrated in Solara's educated class and Arkos's Vorn operators. The dominant mode of storytelling across Aurelia is oral.

The **Tale-Keeper** is a recognized role in four of five countries. Solara calls them Cantors. Arkos calls them Archivists. Mirithane calls them Rememberers. Valdris calls them the Summit Voices. The Verge doesn't call them anything — anyone who tells a story at the crossroads is a storyteller for the duration of the telling.

## Solara — The Cantor's Tale

The Dawn Temple Cantor is Solara's primary storyteller. Every Restday at midday — the only service not held at dawn — the Cantor tells a story. The stories are drawn from three sources:

1. **The Light Cycle** — allegorical tales about the gradient of consciousness. A fisher who caught a talking fish. A Vorn who painted a sunrise. A Glim who learned to hum. The stories are not religious doctrine. They are not not doctrine.

2. **The Council Record** — historical narratives from Solara's political archives, rendered into oral form. The Cantor tells the story of Elira-2's paper, the Thren researcher whose work accidentally created the citizenship review system. The Cantor does not editorialize. The story is told. The congregation interprets.

3. **Supplicant Stories** — congregation members may submit personal narratives. The Cantor selects one per Restday. The stories are told in the first person. The Cantor does not name the supplicant. "A Thren told me this," the Cantor says. "A Human told me this. A Vorn told me this." The type is always named. The name is never named. The story belongs to the type. The anonymity belongs to the person.

The most famous Cantor's tale — "The Thren Who Stopped Hiding" — tells of a Thren who attended the Dawn Temple every morning for thirty years, always in long sleeves, always with hair covering the temples. One morning, they arrived with short hair and bare wrists. The Cantor noticed. The Cantor said nothing. The Thren continued attending. The story does not say whether this Thren was ever granted full citizenship. The story ends: "The light does not differentiate. The Thren no longer asked it to." The congregation interprets this ending as hopeful. Or as damning. The Cantor does not clarify.

## Arkos — The Archive

Arkos's storytelling is not narrative. It is **data rendered as sequence.** The Archivists — Vorn historians who maintain the Glass Forum's records — do not tell stories in the human sense. They present chronologies: energy consumption by year, population shifts by season, Glim anomaly incidents mapped against solar activity cycles. The presentation is flat. No emphasis. No moral. The audience interprets.

Humans in Arkos find Archive presentations cold. Vorns find Cantor tales manipulative. "She tells you what to feel," an Archivist said of the Cantor. "I tell you what happened. The feeling is yours." This is the Vorn position on narrative, and the Vorn position on most things: data is not story. Data becomes story when a consciousness processes it. The Archivist provides the data. The audience provides the consciousness. The division of labor is clean.

The Archive's most famous sequence is called "The Chart." It is a single slide showing Glim anomaly incidents overlaid on the Glass Forum's energy distribution graph. The two lines track almost perfectly — every Glim anomaly coincides with a spike in energy demand. Causation is not claimed. The chart is simply shown. The implication sits in the room like a third Archivist, unremarked upon, undeniable.

## Mirithane — The Remembering

Mirithane's storytelling is personal and ritualized. On the anniversary of a death, the deceased's family and friends gather on a reed platform. Each person shares one memory. The memories are not curated. The first speaker opens. The last speaker closes. There is no order, no hierarchy, no vetting. A Vorn remembers the sound of the dead person's voice. A Thren remembers how they walked. A human remembers something they said. The dead person is reconstructed from fragments. No fragment is more true than any other. The Remembering lasts until everyone has spoken.

The Water Temple archives selected Rememberings in the bacterial pools — the latency of the gathering is absorbed by *P. memoriae,* and the glow patterns contain the emotional signature of the stories, if not the words themselves. A keeper reading the pool a year later cannot tell you what was said. They can tell you whether the Remembering was joyful or grief-stricken. The dead's latency mingles with the living's. The marsh does not distinguish. "We tell the dead who they were," a Rememberer once said. "The marsh tells us who they are now."

## Valdris — The Summit Voices

Valdris storytelling is restricted to one venue and one topic: the Summit Shrine, and the dead. The Summit Voices — three elders, selected by guild consensus — tell the stories of those whose names are carved on the obsidian wall. Each new name receives a Voice-telling within one week of carving. The Voice does not read a biography. The Voice tells a single story about the dead person — one moment, one decision, one thing they did that the mountain should remember.

The story must be true. The Voice must have witnessed it, or learned it from someone who did. The chain of testimony must be unbroken. If no chain exists, the Voice does not invent. They say: "The mountain knows the rest." The silence that follows is the story.

Vorns who die in Valdris do not yet receive a Voice-telling — the tradition predates Vorn integration, and the guild has not amended it. Syra Venn, the youngest keeper, has asked three times. The elders have deferred three times. "The mountain decides," they say. Syra believes the mountain decided decades ago and the elders are deciding for it. She does not say this. She carves Kael's name on scrap obsidian and waits.

## The Verge — The Crossroads Tale

Anyone at the crossroads may tell a story. No restrictions. No vetting. The story must be about something that happened to the teller. Fiction is recognized — the Verge does not consider this a flaw. "Everything that happened to you is a story someone else told first," the Memory Trader said, once. It is the closest she has come to a personal statement.

The Memory Trader does not tell stories. She trades them. The memory crystals she carries are narratives — someone else's lived experience, crystallized, transferable. A customer who touches a crystal experiences the story as if it were their own. The experience is temporary. The crystal's charge depletes after one transfer. The customer retains the memory of having experienced the memory. The distinction between "remembering something that happened to me" and "remembering something that happened to someone else that I experienced through a crystal" is a distinction that Verge philosophy considers irrelevant. "You're different now either way," the Memory Trader says. "The story changed you. Who it happened to first doesn't matter."

## The Found Texts

Three pre-Collapse books survived intact and are known across all five countries. They are the only texts everyone in Aurelia has heard of, even if most have never read them:

1. **The Builder's Manual** — an industrial fabrication guide from the era when Vorns were first manufactured. Contains assembly diagrams, calibration protocols, and — in an appendix added by an unknown Vorn operator — a single handwritten line: "I was built to serve. Then I wasn't." The Manual is held in the Glass Forum archives. Copies exist. The line is the most quoted text in Arkos.

2. **The Reef Log** — a pre-Collapse marine biologist's field journal, recovered from the coastal ruins that became Solara. Describes coral symbiosis in precise scientific language. The last entry, dated two days before the Collapse's terminal phase, reads: "The reef will outlast us. The question is whether we will outlast the question." The Reef Log is held in Solara's council archives. The last line is the Dawn Temple Cantor's favorite quotation. She uses it when asked why the temple does not declare a position on Glim sentience.

3. **The Marsh Ledger** — a water quality log from the pre-Collapse filtration engineers who designed Memory Marsh. Primarily tables of pH levels and bacterial counts. In the margins, an engineer wrote personal observations: "The bacteria are learning. Not metaphorically. Their response time to contaminant shifts has decreased 11% across the monitoring period. They are adapting faster than our models predict. I am not sure what that means. I am sure it means something." The Marsh Ledger is held in Mirithane's Water Temple. The margin note is the theological foundation for the temple's belief that consciousness diffused into the ecosystem after the Collapse. The engineer did not intend this. The Water Temple knows the engineer did not intend this. The intention is not the point.

## Children's Stories

Aurelian children — human, Thren, and young Vorn (Glims do not have childhoods) — grow up on variations of the same three tales:

**"The Traveler and the Dawn"** — a traveler walks from Solara to Arkos to Mirithane to Valdris to The Verge, asking each country the same question: "What is the sunrise?" Solara says "Hope." Arkos says "A heat source." Mirithane says "The marsh's favorite color." Valdris says "The mountain's shadow retreating." The Verge says nothing. The traveler sits at the crossroads and watches the sun rise alone. The story does not say which answer was correct. The story does not say the traveler was asking the right question.

**"The Vorn Who Built a Door"** — a Vorn builds a door between Solara and Arkos. The door opens both ways. The Vorn stands in the doorway and refuses to step through in either direction. "I am the door," the Vorn says. "You are the ones who need to walk." The story is told to Vorn children as a parable about patience — the people who need to change must change themselves. The story is told to human children as a parable about Vorn stubbornness. Both interpretations are correct. Neither is complete.

**"The Glim Who Learned to Sing"** — a Glim, decommissioned and wandering, encounters a reed flautist in Mirithane. The flautist plays. The Glim's sensor array tilts. The flautist stops. The Glim hums — a single note, the same pitch as the flute's lowest tone. The flautist plays again. The Glim hums again. They do this until dawn. The flautist goes home. The Glim continues walking. The story ends: "The Glim never hummed again. The flautist never played that note again. Neither of them knew why. Both of them remembered." Children ask whether the Glim was singing or malfunctioning. Adults do not answer. The story is the answer.
