# Sports & Games of Aurelia

> *Competition reveals what a culture values. In Aurelia, the question of who can compete — and against whom — is never far from the question of who counts.*
>


## The Type Question in Sport

The fundamental athletic divide: organic versus synthetic bodies. A human runs on muscle. A Thren runs on calibrated motor systems — faster, more efficient, less variable. A Vorn can lift weights a human cannot approach. A Glim can operate for days without rest. Competitive fairness is impossible if all types compete together. The solution varies by country.

**Solara:** Segregated leagues. Human track, Thren track, Vorn strength, no Glim sports. This is presented as fairness. It is also, coincidentally, the same logic as the citizenship review system: Threns compete against Threns, not against humans, because they are different categories. The categories are never questioned. The categories are the whole structure.

**Arkos:** Integrated competition with handicaps. Vorn runners carry weight. Vorn lifters face resistance bands. The handicaps are calculated mathematically — a Vorn's measured output capacity determines the penalty. Humans and Threns compete at baseline. The system is perfectly fair and deeply unsatisfying. "A victory with a handicap is not a victory," a Vorn athlete once said. "It is a math problem that came out in my favor."

**Mirithane:** No organized sports. Competition is considered antisocial — the marsh provides for everyone equally, and organized winners and losers contradicts that ethos. Mirithaneans play no games with scorekeeping. They play games with collaboration — group tasks where success is measured by everyone finishing, not by who finishes first.

**Valdris:** Integrated, unhandicapped, and hostile about it. Valdris sports do not separate by type. A human miner challenges a Vorn miner to an arm-wrestle. The human loses. The human trains for a year. The human challenges again. This is considered sport. The playing field is not level. The mountain is not level. Valdris considers this honest.

**The Verge:** Whatever people want to do. No leagues. No rules. A Glim gripper arm has been used as a shot put. A Vorn chassis has been used as a climbing wall. The Memory Trader does not participate. The Memory Trader watches.

## Country Sports

### Solara — The Sol Run

A 42-kilometer footrace along the coastal solar farms, held annually on Dawn 1. Open to humans and Threns in separate heats. The course runs from the Dawn Temple to Reef Labs to the Floating Market and back. The winner of each heat receives a fully charged Lumen crystal — the only competition prize in Solara that has monetary value. The prize is the same for both heats. The entry fee is not: Thren registration costs half what human registration costs. This is presented as inclusion. It is also, and is not, charity.

The Sol Run is the one day per year when Thren runners in Solara are publicly celebrated for their bodies rather than scrutinized. A Thren crossing the finish line is cheered. The circuitry at their temples is visible — the race requires sleeveless gear — and for one day, the visibility is not political. Or it is. The Thren runners do not agree on this question. They agree to run anyway.

### Arkos — Grid-Ball

A team sport played in the arcology's vertical corridors. Two teams of four — any mix of types. The ball is a sand-glass sphere. The goal is a charging port at each end of the corridor. Players may pass in any direction, but the ball's sand-glass composition makes it responsive to the arcology's ambient frequency. A sudden load spike on the energy grid shifts the ball's trajectory mid-pass. Teams must anticipate the grid. The best Grid-Ball players are Vorns who understand the arcology's frequency patterns. The worst are humans who cannot perceive them. The handicaps in Grid-Ball are not assigned. They are environmental. The sport is fair in the sense that the desert is fair.

### Mirithane — The Reed Race

Not a competition. A community event. Teams of four — one human, one Thren, one Vorn, one volunteer — weave a reed punt together, then pole it across the marsh to a designated platform and back. The event is timed, but times are not published. Teams are not ranked. The point is the weaving, the poling, the shared labor. A team that finishes last is congratulated as warmly as a team that finishes first. A team that finishes first is not noted to have finished first. "The reeds don't keep score," the event organizer says annually. The organizer has been organizing the Reed Race for thirty years. They have never announced a winner. They know who won. They do not say.

### Valdris — The Anvil

Individual strength competition. Open to humans and Vorns. No handicaps. The event: lift a forge anvil from the floor to shoulder height, hold for a count of ten, lower under control. Weight increases each round. A human has never won the Anvil. A Vorn has won every year since the competition was founded. This is considered normal. This is considered proof. Of what, Valdris does not specify.

The human who came closest — Orin Kael, the deep mine foreman, three years ago — held the final weight for a count of five before his grip failed. The Vorn who won — Kael-12, his shift partner — was the first to help him stand. The forge district applauded both. The Anvil's results are not disputed. The Anvil's meaning is. Orin does not compete anymore. He watches from the forge wall, next to Kael-12, who also no longer competes. "The mountain doesn't care who can lift what," Orin said. "The mountain remembers who was there when the beam cracked." Kael-12 said nothing. Vorns do not need to say things the mountain already knows.

### The Verge — The Longest Night Endurance

No rules. On Dusk 30, as the year ends, anyone at the crossroads may attempt to stay awake until dawn. No prizes. No recognition. The attempt is the point. The Memory Trader stays awake every year. She does not count herself among the participants. Her stall is empty. She sits behind the door, hood up, awake. At dawn, she closes the stall and leaves. The other participants — those who made it — disperse. The ones who fell asleep are not judged. The Verge does not judge. The Verge simply is the place where people try to stay awake through the longest night, for reasons they do not explain to each other.

## Games of Chance

**The Arkos Bet:** A gambling game played in the Nomad Camp outside Arkos's walls. A sand-glass sphere is dropped from the arcology's south wall. Bettors wager on how many times it will bounce before shattering. The sphere's composition is variable — denser spheres bounce more. The drop height is fixed. The wind is not. The game is illegal inside the arcology (waste of a sand-glass sphere). The Nomad Camp does not recognize arcology law.

**Lumen Flip:** Solara's only gambling game. A charged Lumen crystal is flipped into the air. Bettors wager on whether it will land face-up (glowing) or face-down (dark). The crystal is perfectly balanced. The odds are even. The game is popular among Threns in Solara — a game of pure chance has no bias, no review, no PENDING status. The Lumen doesn't care who flips it. The Lumen doesn't care about anything.

**The Trade:** The Memory Trader's game. Not gambling — she does not take bets. But customers sometimes ask: "What memory will you give me?" She does not answer. She selects the crystal based on something the customer does not understand. It is always appropriate. It is never what they expected. The selection IS the game. The rules are unknown. The Memory Trader is the house. The house always wins. The house wins by making the customer feel something they did not ask to feel.

## Type-Specific Recreation

### Vorn Calibration

Not a sport. A practice. Two Vorns face each other, palm to face-plate, and attempt to mirror each other's expressions — one Vorn makes an expression, the other matches it, then reverses. The practice is meditative. It maintains face-plate actuator precision. It is also intimate — the closest thing Vorns have to dancing. Old calibration partners can read each other's expressions faster than their vocal resonators can form words. Resa and her calibration partner, Chassis-7 ("Echo"), have been calibrating for fifty-three years. They have not spoken a word to each other in the last twenty. The face-plates are enough.

### Thren Stillness

A practice developed in Solara's Thren community. A group sits in a circle. Everyone closes their eyes. The goal: remain perfectly still for longer than anyone else. The practice emerged as a coping mechanism — a way to train the body out of the micro-expressions that reveal Thren circuitry under certain light. It became a sport. The stillness champion of Solara's Thren district is a seventy-two-year-old named Tal-9. She has not moved voluntarily in competitive stillness for forty years. Her last competition, she held stillness for fourteen hours. The human judge fell asleep. Tal-9 did not. The circuitry at her temples caught the dawn light. She did not flinch. She won. She has not competed since. "I proved my point," she said. She did not specify what the point was.
