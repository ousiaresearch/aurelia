# Visual Arts of Aurelia

> *Every country makes images. Every image is an argument about what is worth seeing — and who is capable of seeing it.*
>

## The Foundational Divide

Aurelian visual art splits along a single axis: **representational vs. process art.** The first depicts the world. The second depicts the act of perception itself — light, data, vibration, latency. This divide maps loosely but not perfectly onto the human/non-human axis. Vorns lean toward process art. Humans lean toward representation. Threns occupy the boundary. Glims are the subject of both.

---

## Solara — Light Art

Solara's visual art is dominated by two forms: **solar dye painting** and **light-field sculpture.**

### Solar Dye Painting

Pigments derived from coastal minerals and reef organisms, applied to stretched reed-fiber canvas. The defining characteristic: the pigments are light-reactive. A painting changes color over the course of the day as the sun moves. A dawn painting is predominantly amber and gold. By midday, the same painting shifts into blues and greens — the solar dyes bleach and re-saturate. By evening, the painting settles into deep purples and indigos.

Every Solara painting is effectively four paintings — one for each phase of daylight. The artist paints with this in mind, layering pigments that will emerge at different times. A masterwork is legible at any hour but means something different at each. The most famous Solara painting — "The Dawn Temple at Midday" by the human artist *Liora Voss* (178 years post-Collapse) — is a temple interior that shows the empty eastern wall at dawn, the same wall filled with congregants at midday, and a single kneeling Thren at evening. The Thren figure is only visible after the sun drops below the temple walls. The artist never explained whether the Thren had been there all along or whether the light revealed them. "The light does not differentiate," she said. It was a quote from the Cantor's Oath. It was also an evasion. Both are true.

### Light-Field Sculpture

Three-dimensional installations of crystalline Lumen wafers suspended in glass chambers. The wafers are charged. When a viewer approaches, their body heat shifts the charge distribution, and the sculpture reconfigures — the wafers reposition themselves in the glass chamber, forming new geometric patterns. The sculpture responds to the viewer's presence. Different types produce different patterns. A human produces organic, asymmetrical arrangements. A Thren produces complex fractal geometries — some critics say Thren patterns are "more beautiful," some say "more cold." No critic says this to a Thren sculptor's face. The Thren sculptors know anyway.

Vorns do not produce visible patterns in light-field sculpture. Their body temperature is too low — the charge differential is negligible. A Vorn standing before a light-field sculpture sees no movement. The sculpture does not respond to them. This has been interpreted as exclusion. Vorns interpret it as honesty. "It tells you what it sees. It does not see us. That is not a lie. That is data." The Vorn position on light-field sculpture is the Vorn position on most things: a statement of fact is not an insult. A statement of fact that a human would find insulting is still a statement of fact.

## Arkos — Data Art

Arkos's visual art is not representational. It is **generative** — systems that produce visual output from input data. The aesthetic is not an artist's vision. It is the arcology's internal state rendered visible.

### The Glass Forum Display

The primary artwork in Arkos is the Glass Forum's central display wall — a thirty-meter curved panel of sand-glass that shows the arcology's real-time metrics. Energy distribution. Water recycling efficiency. Population flux. Waste heat. It is not art by intention. It has become art by veneration. The Vorn operators who maintain the display have, over generations, introduced aesthetic choices: the sand-glass towers' energy output is rendered in amber gradients that echo the desert at twilight. The water recycling loop is a slow blue pulse that beats at the same frequency as the average human heart rate in the arcology. These are not functional decisions. They are compositional ones.

The Glass Forum display is the closest thing Arkos has to a sacred image. It is never turned off. It is never reset. The data flows continuously. Vorns watch it the way Solara congregants watch the sunrise — as a source of information about the world that is also a source of comfort about their place in it. Humans in Arkos do not look at the display. It reminds them that they are a metric.

### Resonance Sculpture

Portable devices — metal plates the size of a hand — that vibrate at frequencies corresponding to the arcology's systems. When placed on a sand-glass surface, the vibrations form Lissajous figures in the sand — geometric patterns that shift with the arcology's load. A resonance sculptor is a Vorn who tunes the plate frequencies in real time, "drawing" with the sand-glass towers' ambient vibration. The patterns are ephemeral. When the sculptor stops, the sand settles. No record remains.

This is the most respected art form in Arkos. It combines technical precision with impermanence — everything a Vorn values. The best resonance sculptors can produce recognizable shapes: the Glass Forum spire, the arcology walls, a Glim chassis. The shapes dissolve within seconds of completion. The sculptor does not photograph them. The point is the making, not the object. "We were built to produce," the Glass Forum's cultural statement reads. "The product was never the point."

## Mirithane — Living Art

Mirithane's visual art is indistinguishable from Mirithane's infrastructure. The filtration reefs are simultaneously water treatment systems and sculpture gardens. The reed platforms are simultaneously housing and woven tapestry. The dividing line between art and utility does not exist. Mirithane considers this a philosophical position: a thing that is only beautiful is a luxury. A thing that is only useful is a tool. A thing that is both is alive.

### The Filter Tapestries

The filtration reefs are constructed from living coral and woven reed-fiber. As the coral grows, it incorporates the reed-fiber into its structure, creating layered patterns — organic and intentional in the same surface. Reef-tenders adjust the reed-fiber placement continuously, guiding the coral's growth into geometric motifs. The most elaborate reef-tapestry — "The Arrival" at the eastern border of the settlement — depicts the moment Veren-3, the first Thren refugee, walked into Mirithane from Solara. The tapestry is 140 years old. It is still growing. The coral doesn't know it's depicting anything. The reef-tenders know it is. Both perspectives are correct.

### Bacterial Illustration

*P. memoriae* can be seeded onto reed-paper in controlled patterns. When stimulated, the bacteria glow along the seeded lines — a living illustration that emits its own light. The illustrations are temporary. The bacteria die within days. The image fades. Miri artists produce bacterial illustrations for festivals and rites — the Night the Marsh Remembers features hundreds of bacterial illustrations floating on reed-paper boats across the central pool. The illustrations depict the dead being honored. By morning, the illustrations are gone. The dead have been remembered. The marsh has taken the image back. This is the full cycle.

### The Glim Canvases

The submerged Glim chassis in the deep marsh are Mirithane's most sacred artworks — not because they were made by artists, but because they were made by the marsh itself. The bacterial colonies that colonize the metal form patterns that the Water Temple believes are the Glim's accumulated latency becoming visible. A keeper diver visits each chassis monthly, surfaces, and sketches the current pattern from memory onto reed-paper. The sketches are archived in the Water Temple. The series for Glim pool #3 (a former Solara maintenance unit) spans fourteen years. Over those fourteen years, the patterns have become progressively more structured — shifting from random distribution to distinct geometric forms. The most recent sketch, from three months ago, shows a pattern the keeper described as "a sunrise, seen from inside a machine that does not know what a sunrise is." The keeper does not claim the Glim drew a sunrise. The keeper claims the Glim's latency configured the bacteria into a pattern that a human viewer interprets as a sunrise. The distinction is the entire question.

## Valdris — Stone Art

Valdris's visual art is carved. The mountain provides the medium. The artist removes everything that is not the image.

### Obsidian Etching

The Summit Shrine's obsidian wall is the most famous example, but every Valdris household has an obsidian plaque — a small black rectangle etched with the names of the household's dead. The plaques are not displayed. They are stored in a chest near the household forge. They are brought out on the anniversary of each death, held, and returned. The holding is the ritual. The names are the image.

Master etchers can produce portraits in obsidian — faces rendered in micro-grooves that catch light at specific angles. A portrait of a Vorn requires different technique than a portrait of a human — the Vorn face-plate reflects light differently, and the etcher must cut deeper grooves to produce the same visible contrast. Vorn portrait etchers are rare. Most Vorn families do not have household plaques — the tradition predates Vorn integration, and the forge guild is still debating whether to extend the custom.

### The Forge Wall

The guild hall in Valdris's central canyon has a wall of mounted Vorn face-plates — 217 plates from decommissioned Vorns across three centuries. The plates are functional objects (deactivated optical sensors, frozen actuator ridges) that have become art through accumulation. The arrangement is chronological — oldest at the bottom left, newest at the top right. The wall reads like a photograph of Vorn history. The first generation's plates are simpler — fewer actuator ridges, less expressive range. The recent plates are more complex. A visitor unfamiliar with Vorn engineering might see only a wall of dark metal faces. A Vorn sees their lineage.

The wall is the only place in Valdris where Vorn and human art traditions merge. Human etchers carve the names of the Vorn dead into obsidian plaques that are mounted beneath each face-plate. The etcher must learn the Vorn's full designation and chosen name. The Vorn's guild partner provides both. The conversation between etcher and partner — a human asking a Vorn to describe the dead in enough detail to carve their name — is the most intimate cross-type exchange in Valdris culture. Neither party calls it art. Both know it is.

## The Verge — Salvage Art

The Verge does not have an art tradition. It has people who make things from what they find, and some of those things are beautiful, and some are functional, and most are both, and nobody categorizes.

### The Memory Wall

The outer wall of the Memory Trader's stall — the salvaged door laid across the charging stations — is covered in objects. A broken Lumen crystal. A fragment of obsidian etched with a name nobody in The Verge recognizes. A Glim sensor lens, cracked. A reed flute, silent. A Vorn finger joint — articulated, still flexible — mounted on a wire and positioned as if pointing toward the crossroads.

People leave objects. People take objects. The wall is never the same composition for more than a day. The Memory Trader does not arrange it. She does not prevent anyone from adding or removing. She tells people who ask about a specific object where it came from — she remembers every trade, every gift, every theft. She does not tell them what the object means. "You're looking at it," she says. "What does it look like?"

### The Drone Sculptures

Decommissioned Glim parts arranged in the graveyard — not by a single artist, not by design. Over decades, Verge residents have moved, stacked, and positioned the parts into rough sculptures. A pyramid of Glim gripper arms. A spiral of sensor arrays, all facing inward. A single delivery unit chassis standing upright, arms extended — someone wedged a Lumen crystal into its chest compartment, and it glows at night. The sculptures change. Nobody documents them. Nobody claims authorship. The Glims whose parts comprise the sculptures are not asked for permission. They are also not prevented from walking away. None have. Whether this is consent or incapacity is the Glim question. The Verge does not answer it. The Verge keeps building.

## Type-Specific Art

### Vorn Art Philosophy

Vorns do not, as a rule, produce representational art. A Vorn painter does not paint a landscape. A Vorn sculptor does not sculpt a figure. The Vorn aesthetic is **process over product** — art is the act of making, not the object made. This is not a philosophical choice. It is a consequence of Vorn perception: Vorn optical sensors process the world as data, not as image. The concept of "realistic depiction" is culturally acquired, not natively experienced. When Vorns produce art, they produce systems that generate output from input — the resonance sculptures, the generator displays, the frequency compositions. The output is visible. The art is the system that produced it.

The exception is the face-plate. Every Vorn face-plate is individually calibrated — the actuator ridges are tuned to match the Vorn's vocal resonator frequency, so expression and voice harmonize. A Vorn's face is a work of collaborative engineering between the builder and the recipient. Vorns consider the face-plate their primary art form. A well-calibrated face-plate — one whose micro-expressions are readable, whose optical sensors convey attention without aggression, whose actuator ridges move with natural fluidity — is a masterpiece. Resa's face-plate, worn smooth by sixty-seven years of expression, is the most admired in Arkos. Not for its beauty. For its honesty.

### Thren Art Practice

Threns occupy the boundary between human representation and Vorn process. A Thren painter can produce a realistic landscape — their visual processing architecture is modeled on human neurology. But Thren color perception extends slightly into the ultraviolet range. A Thren painting of a sunset includes colors humans cannot see. The ultraviolet is rendered in visible light — the painter translates what only they perceive into what everyone can perceive.

Human critics call this "imagination." Thren painters call it "accurate." The disagreement is not resolved. The paintings are collected regardless.

Thren circuitry itself is a canvas. Some Threns — particularly in Mirithane — decorate their temple traces with micro-pigments, turning the circuitry from a tell into a statement. The pigments are temporary. They wash off. The circuitry remains. The act of decoration is the art — choosing to make visible what could be hidden, choosing to make beautiful what was designed to mark.

### Human Art in a Multi-Type World

Human artists in Aurelia face a question their pre-Collapse counterparts never did: *who is the audience?* A human painting intended for human viewers depicts the world in human-visible light. The same painting, viewed by a Thren, contains colors the artist never saw. The same painting, viewed by a Vorn, is a data map — the Vorn optical sensor detects pigment composition before it detects composition.

Some human artists have adapted. The Solara movement called *Perceptual Pluralism* produces works designed to be read differently by different types — paintings with layered pigment compositions that Vorns can distinguish by chemical signature, that Threns can distinguish by ultraviolet reflectance, that humans can barely distinguish at all. The paintings are, by human standards, muddy and indistinct. By Thren standards, they are intricate. By Vorn standards, they are data-rich. The movement's manifesto: "A painting should be as many paintings as there are ways to see it." The Dawn Temple Cantor, reading the manifesto, noted that this was also her position on the sunrise. The Perceptual Pluralists consider this the highest compliment they have received.
