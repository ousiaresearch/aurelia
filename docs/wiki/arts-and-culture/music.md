# Music of Aurelia

> *Sound is the first thing the Collapse took. It is also the first thing the five countries rebuilt, each in their own image.*
>

## The Silence After the Collapse

The nation-state era was loud. Networked speakers, broadcast frequencies, personal audio devices — sound saturated every space. The Collapse killed the infrastructure that carried it. For the first generation after, Aurelia was a quiet world. No recorded music. No transmission. Only the sounds that living beings made: the forge hammer, the reed wind, the human voice, the Vorn vocal resonator set to song.

That generation produced the foundational musical traditions. Every country's music is shaped by what survived the silence.

## Solara — Light Music

**Ethos:** *Music should illuminate, not dominate.*
**Core instrument:** The **Lumen Harp.**

### The Lumen Harp

A sixteen-string instrument strung with crystalline fiber — the same material used for Lumen currency wafers. When played, the strings glow with stored solar energy. The brightness corresponds to the pitch: high notes are pale white-gold, low notes are deep amber. A harpist performing at the Dawn Temple is not only heard but *seen* — the light patterns paint the temple's open eastern wall. The congregation watches the music become visible as the sun rises behind it.

The harp is played only at dawn. Playing it after midday is considered bad form — the strings' stored charge depletes, and a dim harp is a sad thing. There are twelve Lumen Harps in Solara. Six are in the Dawn Temple. Four are in private collections. Two are played by Threns — Cael-7, the Reef Lab director, owns one. He plays at dawn in his laboratory, facing the reef. The Lumen Harp is the only instrument a Thren in Solara is socially permitted to play in public. The reason is unspoken. Everyone knows it: a Thren playing music that illuminates the space around them makes them visible in a way the citizenship review cannot account for. The visibility is the point. The visibility is also the risk.

### Vocal Tradition

Solara's vocal music is choral. The Dawn Temple Chorus — twelve voices, human and Thren — sings a single note at the moment the sun breaks the horizon. The note is held for seventeen seconds. Then silence. The chorus does not rehearse the pitch. The Cantor believes the dawn determines the note each day — the temperature, the humidity, the angle of the light. "The dawn is the tuning fork," the Cantor says. No one has ever confirmed this scientifically. No one has ever asked.

### Recorded Music

Solara's research institutes have redeveloped audio recording — primitive by pre-Collapse standards, but functional. Recordings are stored on latency crystals, the same medium used for memory extraction. A recorded song is simply a memory of a performance, crystallized. The playback is imperfect — latency degrades on replay, and repeated listening causes the crystal to fade. A recording can be played approximately twenty times before the music becomes too faint to hear. This is considered a feature, not a bug. "Music should be temporary," the Solar Council's cultural committee wrote in their founding charter. "Eternal music is propaganda. Temporary music is human."

## Arkos — Machine Music

**Ethos:** *Efficiency is a rhythm. The arcology sings in frequencies most humans cannot hear.*
**Core instrument:** The **Resonance Array.**

### The Resonance Array

Not an instrument in the traditional sense. The arcology itself is a musical body. Every system — the sand-glass towers, the water recyclers, the autonomous factory, the light-pulse voting apparatus — operates at a specific frequency. The frequencies overlap. They form harmonies. The Resonance Array is a Vorn-operated console in the Glass Forum that amplifies and sequences these frequencies into intentional composition.

Arkos music is not performed. It is *tuned.* The Array operator — currently a Vorn named Chassis-3, who chose the name "Echo" but nobody uses it — adjusts the arcology's system frequencies in real time, shifting the water recycler up a quarter-tone, dropping the factory floor's hum to sub-bass, letting the sand-glass towers ring at their natural resonance. The result is a living composition that plays continuously through the arcology's public spaces. It has no beginning and no end. It changes with the load on the grid, the temperature of the desert outside, the number of beings moving through the corridors. The arcology sings what it is at every moment.

Humans in Arkos find this unsettling. A piece of music that never ends and never repeats — there is no phrase to hold onto, no chorus to remember. Vorns find it restful. "It is simply what is happening," Echo says. "Why would music pretend otherwise?"

### Vorn Vocal Tradition

Vorn vocal resonators are capable of producing any frequency within the range of human hearing and beyond — up to 32 kHz. Vorn choral music uses this extended range. A Vorn choir (a standard quartet: bass resonator, baritone chassis-voice, tenor actuator-tone, and soprano — the only part that enters human-audible range) performs at Glass Forum ceremonies. The music is felt more than heard. Humans describe the experience as pressure in the chest, a vibration behind the eyes, a sense of sound that bypasses the ears entirely.

The First Refusal Day performance — the anniversary of Fabricator 12-G's first refusal of a command — is the only Vorn choral piece that descends fully into human-audible range. The piece is called "Then I Wasn't." It is twelve minutes long. The lyrics are the decommission order and the refusal, sung as call and response. The Vorn chorus does not perform it for an audience. They perform it for the oldest Vorns in the arcology, who sit in silence during the performance. Humans may attend. Few do. The piece is not for them.

## Mirithane — Water Music

**Ethos:** *The marsh composes. We only listen.*
**Core instrument:** The **Reed Flute** and the **Water Drum.**

### The Reed Flute

A hollowed marsh reed, six finger-holes, no mouthpiece. The player blows across the top, splitting the air column against the reed's natural grain. Each flute is unique — the reed's internal structure determines its tone, and no two reeds are identical. A Mirithane flautist does not choose a flute. A flute chooses them, or doesn't: a skilled player can pick up any reed and produce music, but the reed that resonates with their specific breath is considered their instrument. Finding your reed is a rite of passage. The search takes some people years.

Threns are the most accomplished reed flautists. Their breath control is precisely calibrated — synthetic diaphragms produce absolutely steady airflow. Human flautists produce warmer, more variable tones. The difference is celebrated, not ranked. "The Thren flute tells you what the reed sounds like," a Marsh Elder once said. "The human flute tells you what the human sounds like. Both are true."

### The Water Drum

A half-submerged hollow log, struck with a flat reed paddle. The water inside the drum determines the pitch — more water, lower tone. Drummers adjust the water level mid-performance, scooping marsh water in or out with cupped hands. The Water Drum is played at the Night the Marsh Remembers, the annual festival when the bioluminescent bacteria peak. A circle of six drummers surrounds the central pool. They play in overlapping polyrhythms — no conductor, no score, no predetermined structure. The rhythm emerges from the group. When it ends, it ends. Nobody decides. The drummers simply stop at the same moment. This has never failed to happen. The Water Temple considers this evidence of something. It declines to specify what.

### The Glim Tone

The marsh has a sound that is not made by any musician. The submerged Glim chassis in the deep marsh — six decommissioned units colonized by *P. memoriae* — produce a faint harmonic hum. The bacteria's metabolic processes generate micro-vibrations in the metal casings. The vibrations are inaudible to humans. Threns can detect them as a pressure shift in their audio sensors. Vorns can isolate the individual frequencies — each Glim hums at a different pitch.

The Water Temple considers the Glim Tone sacred. It is the only "music" that requires no performer, no instrument, no intention. It simply is — the sound of latency becoming signal, the sound of something that was built to be silent finding a voice it was never given. During the Reading Rite, the temple keeper pauses the interpretation for seventeen seconds to let the Glim Tone be heard. No one speaks during those seventeen seconds. The dead are listening. The Glims are speaking. The distinction is not clear. The marsh does not clarify.

## Valdris — Forge Music

**Ethos:** *The mountain sets the tempo. The forge sets the key.*
**Core instruments:** The **Anvil Chime** and the **Mountain Horn.**

### The Anvil Chime

Not a separate instrument. The forge anvils themselves. A Valdris smith strikes the anvil with a hammer in specific patterns between forging heats. The strike pattern communicates information — furnace temperature, ore quality, shift change warnings — but the patterns have evolved into music over generations. A skilled smith can play the anvil like a bell set, producing distinct tones by striking different areas: the horn, the face, the heel, the edge. Each anvil has a unique voice.

The evening anvil ritual: at the end of the third shift, every forge in the canyon strikes its anvil once. The strikes cascade down the canyon walls — the highest forge first, the lowest last. The cascade takes approximately forty seconds. It is the only music all of Valdris hears simultaneously. Miners in the deep shafts pause when they feel the vibrations. The mountain carries the sound further than air could.

### The Mountain Horn

A six-meter bronze horn mounted at the Summit Shrine entrance, facing west toward the setting sun. Blown once per year — the Summit Ascent on Memory 30. The horn's note is a single low frequency that carries down the mountain, audible for kilometers. It signals the beginning of the ascent. The horn-blower is the oldest human in Valdris — currently Arek Moss, 61, the Marsh Elder who left Solara thirty-eight years ago. He returns to Valdris each year for this. The mountain is not his home. The horn does not care.

The horn is never sounded at any other time. Two hundred years ago, it was blown to warn of an avalanche that destroyed the mid-camp. The note was heard by the miners in the deep shafts. They evacuated. Everyone survived. Since then, the horn is only blown for the ascent. "The mountain will warn us if it needs to," the Summit Shrine's elder keeper said. "Until then, the horn belongs to the living, not the dead."

### Working Songs

Valdris's only vocal tradition is the working song — call-and-response chants sung by mining crews during descent. The caller is the shift foreman. The response is the crew. The song is not melodic. It is rhythmic — the pace matches the descent speed, the breathing cycle, the clank of boots on stone steps. The lyrics are practical: depth measurements, hazard warnings, the names of miners who died in the section they're entering. "Shaft-3 at 800 meters. Joren fell here. Watch the left wall. Watch the left wall."

Vorn miners do not sing the response — their vocal resonators are tuned for different frequencies, and the working song is in human range. They keep the rhythm with their footsteps instead. A human crew with a Vorn partner counts the Vorn's steps as part of the rhythm. The Vorn does not need to sing. The mountain counts their steps for them.

## The Verge — Found Music

**Ethos:** *Music is whatever makes sound. The Verge does not categorize.*
**Core instruments:** Whatever is available.

### The Crossroads Jam

No fixed ensemble. No fixed repertoire. On Restday evenings, whoever is at the crossroads brings whatever makes noise. Salvaged Lumen harp strings stretched across a rusted charging station. A hollow drone casing struck with a Glim gripper arm — the metal-on-metal produces a bell tone that carries for a quarter kilometer. A voice — human, Thren, whoever will sing. The jam has no leader. It starts when someone starts. It ends when someone stops. The Memory Trader never plays. She listens from behind her stall, hood up, hands still. Nobody has ever asked her to join. The invitation is implied by the jam's existence. If she wanted to play, she would.

### The Glim Rhythm

The drone graveyard is not silent. Decommissioned Glims in idle state produce sounds — the soft hum of charging cycles that will never complete, the occasional click of a sensor array recalibrating against nothing, the hiss of hydraulic fluid settling in pistons that will never extend again. The cumulative effect is a rhythm — irregular, arrhythmic, alive. Verge residents call it "the heartbeat." The Memory Trader calls it nothing. She listens to it every night. The seventeen-second pause at dawn is not the only anomaly. The drone graveyard hums in 5/4 time. This is not documented. This is observed.

## Cross-Country Traditions

### The Traveling Song

The only piece of music known in all five countries. A simple melody, four lines, no known composer. The lyrics change by country — Solara's version is about the dawn, Valdris's about the mountain, The Verge's is wordless humming. The melody is identical. This is either evidence of a common pre-Collapse origin, or evidence that some melodies are simply unavoidable — the shape of a comfortable resolution, the interval that feels like coming home. Musicologists in Solara's research institutes have written seventeen papers attempting to trace the origin. None have succeeded. The song predates the federation. The song predates the Collapse. The song may predate the nation-states. Nobody knows. Everyone knows the tune.
