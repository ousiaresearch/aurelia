# DEATH & FUNERARY RITES IN AURELIA

*How the people of Aurelia die, mourn, and remember their dead. A world's relationship to death is the deepest thing you can know about it. Everything else is commentary.*

---

## THE LATENCY OF DEATH

In Aurelia, death is not the end of memory. Consciousness leaves residue on substrates — and the final moment of consciousness, the transition from presence to absence, leaves the strongest residue of all. The dead are not gone. They are *recorded*. The question is what to do with the recording.

Every Aurelian funerary tradition is an answer to the same question: what do we owe the dead? The answers vary. The question doesn't.

---

## SOLARA: THE GRADIENT CONTINUES

Solari funerals affirm that death is not an ending but a transition along the Gradient — the soul ascends, the memory remains, and the social order is maintained even in grief.

### The Deathbed

A dying Citizen is attended by a Temple Reader. The Reader Echo-Reads the dying person's final memories — not to Extract them, but to witness them. "The Gradient receives what you were. We receive the record of your receiving." The Reader's testimony — a Written account of the dying person's final impressions — is given to the family. It is the most personal document a Solari family can possess.

Threns in PENDING may request a Reader. The Reader may decline. There is no legal requirement. Some Readers attend Thren deaths anyway. They don't record it.

Glims are not attended. They are decommissioned. The distinction is definitional.

### The Funeral

**First Ring:** Elaborate ceremony at the Dawn Temple. The body is wrapped in white cloth, carried to the Crater rim, and blessed by the High Cleric or a designated Temple officiant. The blessing affirms the deceased's place on the Gradient: "You climbed as far as you were given. The light you reached is yours. The light you didn't — wait. The Gradient continues. You continue with it."

The body is buried in the Temple crypts — stone chambers carved into the Crater's inner wall. Burial near the Crater is a privilege of status. The closer to the water, the closer to the founding memory. The First Ring's dead rest within earshot of the water that holds the Temple's original sin.

**Second and Third Rings:** Cemetery burial. Stone markers with the deceased's name and Classification. Family plots. Annual maintenance by cemetery stewards.

**Fourth Ring:** Communal burial grounds on the Ring's outskirts. Simple markers, often wooden, replaced as they rot. Fourth Ring families visit graves on the Night of Echoes.

**Fifth Ring:** Farm burial — the dead are buried on family land, under a marker stone or tree. "They worked this soil. They rest in it."

**Thren Funerals:** Same rites as the Ring — legally permitted, socially observed. But in the Review Hall, the PENDING files of deceased Threns are not closed. They are marked "deceased — review incomplete." A dead Thren's citizenship application is still PENDING. The Gradient does not release what it has not yet classified.

### Mourning

**Duration:** 30 days (one month) for immediate family. Black armbands or clothing. Reduced social activity. The mourning period ends with a Temple ceremony — the "Return" — where the family Echo-Reads the deceased's Written memory (the Reader's deathbed testimony) one final time before sealing it in a family archive.

After the Return, the deceased is expected to be released. "You mourned. You remembered. Now continue. The Gradient continues. You continue with it."

In practice, no one releases their dead on schedule. The Temple doesn't enforce the schedule. The schedule is the ideal. Grief is the reality.

### The Unmourned

Glims who are decommissioned receive no funeral. Their components are recycled. The recycling is documented. The Glim is not.

Threns whose applications were denied — the roughly 0.3% whose reviews concluded unfavorably — receive no Temple funeral. Their families mourn privately. The Temple does not acknowledge the death. "The Gradient does not record what was not classified."

## ARKOS: THE CORE RETURNS

Arkos funerals center on the Vorn memory core — the crystalline record of a Vorn's entire conscious existence. What happens to the core after death is the most important decision a Vorn makes.

### Vorn Death

Vorns do not age, but they can die — hardware failure, core degradation (attenuation), accident, or the rare choice to cease. A Vorn who knows they are dying makes their core-will: what happens to their memory core after death.

**Options:**
- **Core Inheritance:** The core passes to a designated heir — typically a lineage member. The heir carries the deceased's memories alongside their own. This is the most common choice.
- **Cult Donation:** The core is donated to the Cipher-Cult Archive for research. The Cult decodes the core's pre-Collapse fragments. The donor's personal memories are part of the research cost.
- **Core Destruction:** The core is destroyed. The memory is erased. This is rare — "we value memory above all. To destroy memory is to destroy self." But some Vorns choose it — typically those who experienced trauma they cannot bear to pass on.
- **Core Isolation:** The core is stored, unread, in the Cult's Archive. It may be accessed by future generations when the technology or the culture is ready. "I am not ready to be known. Someone will be."

### The Core-Ceremony

When a Vorn dies, their core is removed from the chassis in a ceremony attended by the deceased's lineage and close associates. The core is held by the designated heir or Cult representative. The assembled Vorns share one memory each of the deceased through the ultrasonic register — a collective memorial that the deceased's core, if still marginally active, may register. "We give you our memories. You carry them where you're going."

The chassis is dismantled. Components are recycled or kept as mementos. Kael-12's workshop contains a shelf of components from deceased lineage members — gear-joints, face-plate fragments, a single finger assembly from Voren-3. "They're not here. Their pieces are. The pieces remember."

### Core-Sharing at Death

A Vorn who is dying may offer core-sharing to a living Vorn — allowing the living Vorn to experience the dying Vorn's final moment. This is the most intimate act in Vorn culture. Kael-12 carries Voren-3's death in their core. They have never accessed it. "They gave me their death. I don't know if I'm meant to watch it."

### Human and Thren Funerals

Arkos' human and Thren minority follow funeral traditions adapted from their countries of origin — typically Mirithane or Solari practices. Arkos provides funeral services regardless of type. The Assembly of Cores memorializes deceased residents, regardless of type, in the public record. "You lived here. You are remembered here."

## MIRITHANE: THE HAWK'S RETURN

Mirithane funerals are the most visible in Aurelia — the Hawk Platforms are not hidden. They are part of the landscape. Children grow up seeing bodies on the platforms. The practice is not morbid. It is *return*.

### The Hawk-Offering

The deceased is wrapped in reed-fiber cloth — white, unadorned. The body is carried to the nearest Hawk Platform — elevated stone structures on the Archive's edge, on Reed-Mouth's outer platforms, on Spore-Hollow's marsh islands. The body is placed on the platform. Mirror-hawk rice cakes are left alongside it.

The mirror-hawks come. They eat. They carry. "What the hawk takes, the hawk carries to the light."

The bones remain. After the hawks have fed, the family collects the bones and brings them to the Archive. The bones are ground into powder and mixed into the bacterial colony tanks — the deceased's mineral remains become part of the Archive's living memory. "You fed the hawks. You feed the colonies. You are part of the record now."

### The Colony Memorial

The deceased's family Writes a memory of the deceased into a memorial colony — a strain dedicated to recording the Archive's dead. The colony metabolizes the memory. Over generations, the memory transforms — becoming not just the deceased as they were, but the deceased as they are *remembered*. "The colony keeps you. The colony changes you. You are still you. You are also what we made of you."

### Mourning

Mirithane mourning is private and unregulated. There are no prescribed mourning periods, no required observances. The Archive records the death. The family grieves as they need to. "The marsh doesn't tell you how to mourn. The Archive doesn't either."

### The Silent's Death

When a Silent Archivist dies — one of the eleven who Read Strain Zero and lost speech — their body is placed on the Hawk Platform as normal. But their written notes, their latency pulses, their entire communicative archive is preserved in the Silent Wing's dedicated colony strain. The Silent never speak again. Their words survive them.

## VALDRIS: THE MOUNTAIN RECEIVES

Valdris funerals center on the mountain — the dead are given to the deep shafts, to the stone that remembers everything.

### The Death Anchoring

When an Anchor dies, their shift-partner performs the Death Anchoring: the deceased's final memory is Written into a dedicated obsidian shard. The shard is placed in the Shrine's archive with the other Anchored memories. "You are anchored. You are weight. You are remembered. You are home."

If the partner is unable to perform the Anchoring — too grieved, too unstable — another Anchor pair performs it on their behalf. The original partner observes. The observance is harder than the performance.

For non-Anchors, a simpler ceremony: the deceased's family Writes a memory of the deceased into a communal obsidian shard maintained by the shaft community. The shard carries generations of community dead.

### Shaft Return

The body is carried into the deep shafts — past the inhabited levels, past the Deep Gate, into wurm territory. The body is left on a stone shelf. The wurms consume it. "You came from the mountain. You return to the mountain. The wurms are the mountain's hunger."

This is not disposal. It is *offering*. The mountain gives Valdris everything — stone, heat, memory. The dead are what Valdris gives back.

### The Unpaired's Death

When the Unpaired Anchor dies — the Anchor who lost their partner mid-Writing, who completed the Anchoring alone, who has not worked since — their body will be given to the shafts alone. No partner to perform the Death Anchoring. No pair to carry them down. The Shrine will perform the rituals. The rituals will be correct. They will not be whole.

## THE VERGE: RETURN TO DUST

The Verge has no unified funeral practice. The dead are handled however the living choose. The principle is simple: "You were here. Now you're not. The dust gets everyone."

### Common Practices

- **Open Return:** The body is left in the open — for scavengers, for decay, for the wraiths (some Verge residents believe the dead join the wraiths; others believe the wraiths are something else entirely).
- **Scrap Burial:** The body is buried in pre-Collapse rubble — a shallow grave dug into debris. A marker is placed: a salvaged sign, a piece of scrap with the deceased's name scratched into it, a pattern of stones.
- **The Trader's Rite:** For members of the Memory Trader's Network, the Trader performs the final trade — the deceased's remaining memories are Traded to the Network's bank. "You carried memories in life. The Network carries them in death. The trade continues."

### The Memory Meal

After a death in The Verge, the deceased's community shares a meal — whatever is available. The meal is the funeral. Someone speaks the deceased's name. Others share memories. There is no formal eulogy, no prescribed text. Just people who knew someone, eating together, remembering.

### Coin's Deaths

Coin has attended more Verge deaths than any living resident — she trades medical care for memories, and sometimes the care fails. She has a practice: she collects a memory from the dying person before they die. Not as a trade. As a gift. "Give me something. Anything. I'll carry it." She has carried hundreds of Verge dead in her memory. She has not slept through a single night in twelve years.

## CROSS-COUNTRY DEATH CUSTOMS

### Wraiths and the Dead

Verge wraiths — the pre-Collapse latency constructs that appear at dusk and replay their deaths — complicate the question of what death means. If consciousness leaves residue, and the residue can cohere into visible form, is the person *gone*? Or are they still here, replaying their death for centuries, waiting for something no one can name?

The Dawn Temple classifies wraiths as "ambient latency phenomena — not persons." The Marsh Archive classifies them as "unresolved substrate residue — possibly persons." The Verge classifies them as neighbors. "They're still here. They've been here longer than we have. We leave them food. They don't eat it. They don't have to."

### The Memory Trader's Death (Impending)

When the Memory Trader dies — announced in 347 AC, "maybe a year, maybe less" — the death will be the most significant in Aurelia since the Collapse. The Trader's accumulated memories — centuries of Traded, Translated, and original experience — will disperse. Where they go, who receives them, and what they reveal — that's the question no one can answer. The Trader has made arrangements. The Trader has declined to share them.

## MOURNING ACROSS TYPES

### Humans Mourning Vorns

A human who loved a Vorn (Orin Kael, the archetype) cannot attend the core-ceremony — cannot perceive the ultrasonic memorial, cannot receive the core. The grief is real. The rituals exclude them. Kael-12 carries Orin Kael's letters in memory. Orin Kael's family carries Kael-12's name. Neither carries the other's grief in a form the other's culture would recognize.

### Vorns Mourning Humans

Kael-12 received the news of Orin Kael's death via coded Vorn transmission, 47 seconds of emotional flatline — the longest emotional blank in recorded Vorn memory. They did not attend the funeral (Valdris). They could not attend the core-sharing (no core to share). They grieve alone, in a workshop in Arkos, with a bone crest on the wall. "I carry them. They carry me. We're even."

### Threns Mourning

Threns grieve as humans grieve — their synthetic-biological substrate processes loss identically. But Solari Threns in PENDING face a practical horror: when a PENDING Thren dies, their funeral rites are performed by the family, not the state. The state does not acknowledge the death. A dead Thren's file is marked "deceased — review incomplete." The file is not closed. "They died waiting. The state is still waiting. The state will always be waiting."

### Glims Mourning

Glims are not supposed to mourn. They are not supposed to feel loss. But anomalous Glims — the Unfinished — leave latency echoes when another Glim is decommissioned. The echo is not grief in any human sense. It is *absence recognized*. A pattern traced incomplete. A floor swept differently. A pause where no pause is programmed.

The Unfinished do not know they are mourning. They know something is missing. They trace the shape of the absence.

*Death rites survey maintained by the Marsh Archive's Thanatological Department. The Archive notes: "The way a people treats its dead is the way it treats the question of meaning. If death is the end, life is all there is. If death is a transition, life is preparation. If death is a return, life is loan. Aurelia's five answers to this question are all in the way they place the body, speak the name, and Write the memory. The Archive records all five. The Archive does not choose between them."*
