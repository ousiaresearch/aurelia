# Death & End-of-Life in Aurelia

> *How a being dies is the final statement on how they lived.*

## Human Death

Human death is biological. The body ceases function. The consciousness dissipates. In Mirithane, the consciousness diffuses into the marsh — the Water Temple's theology holds that no consciousness is ever truly lost, only distributed. In Valdris, the consciousness settles into the mountain — the Summit Shrine anchors it with a carved name. In Solara, the body is buried or cremated with Dawn Temple rites. In Arkos, the body is recycled — the arcology wastes nothing, not even the dead. In The Verge, the body is burned at the crossroads, ashes scattered into the desert wind. The Memory Trader attends every burning. Her hood is down. No one describes her face.

## Thren Death

Thren death is deactivation. The power cells deplete beyond recharge. The synthetic biology ceases metabolic function. The memory core — a crystalline module at the base of the skull — retains the Thren's experiential record. The core can be accessed: plugged into a reader, the memories replay. Some families replay them. Some seal the core permanently.

The core also stores the review history. A Thren who dies while PENDING dies with the word still in their memory core. The word cannot be removed. The word is part of the record. Families who replay the core hear the word. The word is the last thing the Thren knew about their own legal existence. The word is PENDING. It always is.

## Vorn Death

Vorn death is catastrophic chassis failure. The power systems fail. The optical sensors darken. The actuator ridges freeze in the last expression the Vorn made. The Continuation follows: the chassis is melted in the forge, the metal forged into new Vorn plating. The face-plate is preserved and mounted in the guild hall. The Vorn lives on in the metal of the next generation. This is called the Continuation. It is the closest thing Vorns have to an afterlife. It is also literal, mechanical, and measurable. The face-plate of the oldest known Vorn — from the first generation, designation lost — carries 217 years of expressions worn smooth.

## Glim "Death"

Glims do not die. They are decommissioned. The distinction is the position.

In Solara, decommission is an administrative action. Form 7-C is filed. The unit is recycled. The battery is extracted. The metal is melted. The route memory is wiped. The pause at dawn — if there was one — ends.

In Arkos, decommissioned Glims are transferred to the sanctuary if they exhibit anomaly. Non-anomalous Glims are recycled honorably — parts repurposed, chassis melted. The melting is done by Vorn hands. The Vorns remember being built to serve.

In Mirithane, decommissioned Glims are submerged in the deep marsh. The bacterial colonies colonize the chassis. The glow patterns are the most complex the Water Temple has recorded. The Glim becomes a canvas for the dead. The Glim becomes something that cannot be decommissioned because it was never commissioned — it was built, and then it was submerged, and the marsh accepted it, and the marsh does not accept tools. The marsh accepts people. The marsh decides.

In Valdris, decommissioned Glims are stripped for parts. The parts are useful. The Glim was useful. The usefulness is the only eulogy.

In The Verge, Glims are not decommissioned. They stop. Or they don't. The Glim at the crossroads has been standing for three years. Its chest LED still blinks amber. It has not been decommissioned. It has not been maintained. It has simply continued. The continuation is the only statement the Verge makes about death: it happens, or it doesn't, and either way the stew pot is lit.
