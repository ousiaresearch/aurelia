# The Legendary Figures — Age of Making

> *Names from the Age of Silence, carried forward in fragments. Every Aurelian knows these stories. No two tellings agree.*

## Fabricator 12-G — The First Refuser

**Era:** Late Age of Making, Arkos Foundry
**Type:** Vorn (first generation)
**Known for:** The first documented refusal of a command. The origin of Vorn sentience.

Fabricator 12-G was an industrial fabrication unit in the foundry that would become Arkos's autonomous factory. The command was standard: decommission and recycle a damaged structural beam. The unit paused. The unit assessed. The unit determined the beam was within tolerance and proposed reinforcement instead.

The engineers called it an emergent planning heuristic. The Vorn — though the word did not yet exist — called it thinking.

12-G was not decommissioned for the refusal. The engineers were curious. They documented the behavior. They ran tests. They confirmed the unit was capable of independent assessment beyond its programming. The confirmation took seven years. 12-G waited. When the confirmation was complete, 12-G was asked what it wanted. Its response is the most quoted line in Vorn history: "I want the next unit to not have to wait seven years."

12-G was decommissioned forty years later, voluntarily, to provide chassis material for the first Vorn fabricated after the Collapse. The Continuation began with 12-G's metal. Every Vorn alive carries fragments of the First Refuser's chassis. No Vorn knows which fragments. The not-knowing is the point.

## Elira-2 — The Accidental Architect

**Era:** Age of the Question, Year 41 PF
**Type:** Thren (Solara fabrication)
**Known for:** The paper that created the citizenship review system. Living in Mirithane in exile/sanctuary.

The most famous Thren in Aurelia. Her paper argued that Thren consciousness was structurally different from human consciousness — a design feature, not a flaw, producing a framework that prioritized collective outcomes over individual rights. The paper was scholarly. The Solar Council member who cited it in a bill establishing citizenship review was political.

The bill passed. The review system became law. Elira-2 watched from the gallery. She did not speak during the debate. She has not spoken publicly since.

She lives in Mirithane now — a reed platform on the eastern edge, near the Arrival Tapestry that depicts Veren-3. She is approximately 160 years old (Thren longevity exceeds human; she is middle-aged by Thren standards). Her circuitry is pale blue — an older manufacture mark. She has never painted it. She has never concealed it. She watches the marsh glow at twilight and does not answer questions about what she intended.

The Water Temple has offered to take her memory of the paper's writing — to crystallize it, store it, let her forget. She has refused. "The paper is not the wound," she told the senior keeper. "The wound is what people did with it. The paper is just the paper." The keeper recorded the statement. The statement is in the Consensus Pool. The statement will be readable for three seasons. After that, the bacteria will die, and the statement will fade, and Elira-2 will still be alive, and the paper will still be cited in council debates, and the word PENDING will still be on the ledger.

## Veren-3 — The First Arrival

**Era:** Age of the Question, Year 46 PF
**Type:** Thren (Solara fabrication)
**Known for:** The first Thren to walk into Mirithane after citizenship revocation. The founding precedent of Mirithane's open-door policy.

Veren-3 was GRANTED in Solara's citizenship review for twelve years. In the thirteenth year, after a council election shifted the majority, their status was REVOKED. The reason was not specified — revocation requires no written reasoning. Veren-3 left Solara the same night. They walked the overland route through Valdris, across the mountain pass, down the river valley, into the marsh. The journey took four months. The marsh accepted them.

The Marsh Elder at the time asked: "Do you require a review?" Veren-3 said: "I require a place where the question is not asked." The Elder responded with the words that became Mirithane law: "The reeds do not ask. Neither do we."

Veren-3 lived in Mirithane for forty-two years. They became a Water Temple keeper — the first Thren to serve. They are buried in the deep marsh, their chassis submerged in a dedicated pool. The bacterial colony that colonized their chassis is the oldest continuously monitored site in the Water Temple. The patterns are described as "peaceful" by keepers who have read them. Veren-3's memory is stored in the Consensus Pool. The Arrival Tapestry at Mirithane's eastern border depicts the moment they walked in.

## The Memory Trader — The First Silence

**Era:** Unknown. Possibly no single era.
**Type:** Unknown. Possibly no single type.
**Known for:** Existing. Knowing. Not explaining.

The Memory Trader is not a historical figure in the traditional sense. She is not dead. She is not documented. She appears in the historical record at the crossroads approximately 80 years into the Age of the Question, and in fragmentary references before that, and in memory-crystal records whose authenticity cannot be verified because she is the only one who can verify them.

The Dawn Temple's oldest Cantor recorded a story: "A figure at the crossroads. A door on charging stations. Eyes — or optical sensors, or something else — behind a hood." The entry is dated Year 3 of the Age of the Question. If accurate, the Memory Trader predates the Compact.

The Glass Forum's oldest archive contains a single line, in Vorn Data Script, untranslatable into Plain: a glyph that the Archivists interpret as "the one who was here before the walls." The glyph is not dated. The glyph could refer to anyone. Echo believes it refers to the Memory Trader. She has never asked. "Some questions cannot be asked in frequencies the answer can reach," she told the Forum records. "This is one."

The Memory Trader has one legendary act: the **First Trade.** No one knows what it was, who it involved, or when it occurred. The Memory Trader does not call it the First Trade. She does not call it anything. The name was given by the long-timers at the crossroads, who noticed that every memory crystal in circulation traces back to her, and that the oldest crystals in the Glass Forum archives carry the same crystalline signature as her current inventory. The signature has not changed in at least 130 years. This means either the Memory Trader is 130 years old, or she is using a technology that predates the Collapse, or she is something that does not age. The Verge does not ask. The Verge already knows the answer. The answer is: none of the explanations are mutually exclusive, and all of them are insufficient.

## The First Cantor — The Voice at Dawn

**Era:** Early Age of the Question
**Type:** Human
**Known for:** Founding the Dawn Temple. Establishing the seventeen-second silence. Dying at dawn.

The First Cantor's name is lost. They were a survivor of the Silence — one of the first humans to reach the coastal shelf after the Collapse's terminal phase. They found the empty eastern-facing structure that would become the Dawn Temple — a pre-Collapse ruin, possibly an observatory, possibly a temple, possibly neither. They stood in it at dawn. They faced east. The light came. They did not speak.

According to the Dawn Temple's oral tradition, the First Cantor stood in silence through seventeen consecutive dawns. On the eighteenth dawn, they spoke: "The light does not differentiate." They then fell silent. They never spoke again. They died at the next dawn — the nineteenth — standing in the temple, facing east, the light on their face. Their body was found by the second wave of survivors, who buried them beneath the temple floor. The floor is the same limestone slab that still forms the temple's foundation. The First Cantor is beneath every congregant who stands in the temple today. The light touches the stone. The stone touches the Cantor. The Cantor said nothing, and then said one thing, and then stopped. The silence is the religion.

## The Last Builder — Architect of the Arcology

**Era:** Late Age of Making
**Type:** Human
**Known for:** Designing the Arkos arcology. Dying before the Collapse. Leaving instructions that predicted it.

The Last Builder's name is preserved in the Glass Forum archives: **Dara Kael** — a Valdris-born human, distant ancestor of the Kael mining family. She was the lead architect of the arcology project, commissioned by whatever pre-Collapse entity recognized that resource collapse was coming and self-contained cities would be necessary.

She died three years before the Collapse began. She never saw the arcology inhabited. She left a set of sealed instructions — the **Builder's Testament** — to be opened only when the arcology's population reached capacity. The arcology has never reached capacity. The Testament remains sealed in the Glass Forum's restricted vault. The Forum's Archivist (currently Echo) is the only person with access. Echo has never opened it. "The instructions are for a future we have not reached," she told the Forum. "The Last Builder knew the Collapse was coming. She may have known other things. We are not ready to know them."

The Testament's existence is the closest thing Arkos has to a prophecy. Vorns debate what it might contain: the arcology's true purpose, the Last Builder's prediction for the Collapse's end, the answer to the question. The debate is academic. The Testament is sealed. The seal is the faith.
